Why your intuition is right
Retrieval-augmented generation, as it actually works in 2026, ingests text more or less as-is. The pipeline is straightforward:

Chunking — the system automatically splits your essays, lectures, sermons, fellowship transcripts into segments of (say) 500–1500 tokens. No human decisions required. Some chunkers respect paragraph boundaries; some use overlapping windows; the choice barely matters for serviceable systems.
Embedding — each chunk gets converted to a vector representation by an embedding model. Automated.
Indexing — chunks + vectors go into a vector database. Automated.
Retrieval at query time — when someone asks a question, the question gets embedded, and the system finds the chunks whose vectors are closest in semantic space. Automated.
Generation — Claude (or whichever frontier model is the backbone) gets the retrieved chunks plus the role prompt plus the user's question, and produces an answer.

Notice what's missing from this pipeline: every single thing Copilot's template asks you to hand-tag. Cross-references, reasoning scaffolds ("use this chunk when the user asks about…"), contradiction maps, ontology placement, argument maps — none of it is needed at ingestion time. The retrieval step finds relevance from semantic similarity at query time, on the fly, against the actual user's question. You cannot pre-anticipate every question well enough to tag chunks with retrieval hints; the system does this better than you can, by working from the question that was actually asked.
So Copilot is not wrong that the elaborate template would work. It's wrong that the elaborate template is necessary. It's confusing two genuinely different kinds of AI infrastructure — a hand-curated structured knowledge graph (which is expensive, slow, brittle, and rare) versus a RAG corpus over organic text (which is cheap, fast, robust, and standard). The preprocessing template is specified for the first kind of system; you want the second kind.
What's actually doing the work in Copilot's template
Strip away the elaborate scaffolding and three things in Copilot's template do real work:
Topic tags and metadata (date, source, author, audience level, paper type). These are useful — but you already have them, in YAML frontmatter, by virtue of the document conventions in the RM repo. You did not need a special preprocessing pass to produce them; you produced them organically through good document hygiene.
The paper-level summary (300–500 words capturing the thesis and how it fits into the broader corpus). This is useful — and it can be auto-generated at ingest time by having Claude read the document and produce the summary as a structured comment, automatically, at API-token cost rather than human-hour cost. You don't write it; the system writes it for you.
The "gold-standard answers" set — the 20–50 hand-crafted Q&A pairs the development_arc and vision docs both mention. This is the one piece of human curation that genuinely cannot be automated and that genuinely matters. These pairs serve three purposes: they're the evaluation harness for the RAG system (does the system reproduce my voice on canonical questions?), they're available as few-shot examples in prompts (giving the model concrete templates of how Thomas reasons), and they become high-priority training data if and when fine-tuning is added later. But that's a much smaller deliverable than processing every paper through the elaborate template — it's 20–50 hand-written answers, not 1,000 papers × 10 metadata fields each.
The inversion that's worth noticing
Here is something interesting about Copilot's template that I think is worth saying out loud: the preprocessing work it describes would actually make a wonderful human study aid, even though it's the wrong thing to do for AI training. Atomic chunks with explicit dependencies, contradiction maps, argument maps, canonical definitions, ontology placement — all of that is what a serious student of CPP and RM would build to teach the material. So if you (or someone) ever wanted to invest that effort, the right product is a study curriculum or a Christos-Seminar reading guide, not Thomas-AI training data. The work is real work; it's just been mis-labeled. That's a useful thing to know going forward when someone proposes elaborate corpus engineering: ask whether the deliverable is for humans or for the AI, because the right structure differs.
