# Hating Evil, Loving the Captured

## A Christos Civitas Code of Relational Ethics — The Four-Component Framework

**Fellowship Discussion Essay | April 28, 2026**

**Occasion:** This essay is the deeper development of the relational-ethics framework first sketched in [Truth in Love, Across the Lines That Divide](https://github.com/Renaissance-Ministries/RM/blob/main/Fellowship_essays/260428-truth_in_love_kirschner.md), which engaged Dr. Rick Kirschner's social-media reflection on the fracturing of American discourse and his proposed book *False Friends and Fractured Professionals.* The first essay was calibrated as a bridge-building piece — engaging Kirschner as a fellow-traveler reaching largely the same conclusions through natural-law warrant, and articulating a three-component Christian alternative to the contemporary collapse of generosity in disagreement. This second essay does the deeper theological work the first essay sketched but did not fully develop. It articulates a four-component framework that takes seriously what the first essay treated too lightly: that the Christian moral universe contains legitimate objects of hatred, that correction is participation in God's labor rather than optional kindness, and that listening is theological discipline rather than mere pastoral courtesy.

**Context:** This is the second essay in the Christos Civitas relational-ethics treatment. Where the first essay was calibrated for Kirschner as a Jewish cultural-conservative interlocutor, this one is calibrated for the fellowship's own internal use — though Rick is welcome to read it and would, I think, find much in it he could affirm through his own warrant, even where the spiritual-warfare framing is foreign to his idiom. The framework articulated here is what the Christos Civitas Code of Relational Ethics would actually contain if developed as the book the first essay proposed.

---

To the Fellowship —

The first essay on Rick's question stopped short of where the question actually leads. It named that the convinced Christian holds firm convictions, that loving the person matters more than insisting on the position, and that the certainties must be distinguished from the prudential judgments. All of that is true. None of it is enough.

What the first essay did not say plainly is that there are objects of legitimate hatred in the Christian moral universe, that the failure to hate them is itself a corruption, and that the relational ethic the apostolic deposit teaches is not the absence of hatred but the *proper direction* of hatred — toward the spiritual evil rather than toward the person captured by it. The first essay also did not say plainly that listening is not just courtesy but theological discipline, required by the task of spirit-discernment that the apostolic deposit explicitly commands. And the first essay did not say plainly that correction of the captured person is not optional kindness but participation in God's own labor — because if God experiences the universe through the inside of every person He has made, then the person captured by evil is the one through whom God Himself is bearing that evil from the inside, and our indifference to the captivity is indifference to God's own experiential burden.

This essay says those things plainly. The framework it articulates has four components rather than three, and the whole framework is sharper-edged than the first essay's version. It will read more as spiritual warfare than as civic disagreement-management, because that is what the apostolic deposit names as the actual situation. *We wrestle not against flesh and blood, but against principalities, against powers, against the rulers of the darkness of this world, against spiritual wickedness in high places* (Ephesians 6:12). The relational ethic the apostolic deposit teaches is the ethic of soldiers in a real war, not diplomats negotiating differences of taste. Soldiers in a real war love their comrades, fight to rescue the captured, hate the enemy that has captured them, and never confuse the captured person with the enemy that holds him. That is the discipline.

I will engage the framework in seven movements. First, why the language of spiritual warfare is not metaphorical but descriptive. Second, the proper direction of hatred — what is to be hated, by whom, and why the failure to hate it is a corruption rather than a virtue. Third, the proper direction of love — toward the person as image-bearer, captured and to be rescued, in whom God Himself is bearing the captivity from the inside. Fourth, listening as theological discipline — not just pastoral kindness but the spirit-discernment task the apostolic deposit explicitly commands. Fifth, the taxonomy of certainties / strong inferences / prudential judgments, which the first essay introduced and which this essay sharpens. Sixth, the four-component framework as a whole, with the relationship among the components named. Seventh, application to the fellowship's actual situation — Leonard, the political moment, and the cultural moment we are all living through.

## I. Why Spiritual Warfare Is Not Metaphorical

Begin with what the apostolic deposit actually says.

*Put on the whole armour of God, that ye may be able to stand against the wiles of the devil. For we wrestle not against flesh and blood, but against principalities, against powers, against the rulers of the darkness of this world, against spiritual wickedness in high places* (Ephesians 6:11-12). This is Paul writing to actual believers in actual congregations, telling them that the enemy is not the human beings around them but the spiritual powers that operate behind and through those human beings. The believer who treats the conflict as merely human-versus-human has misidentified the situation and is fighting the wrong battle.

*The weapons of our warfare are not carnal, but mighty through God to the pulling down of strong holds; casting down imaginations, and every high thing that exalteth itself against the knowledge of God, and bringing into captivity every thought to the obedience of Christ* (2 Corinthians 10:4-5). Paul is naming again that the warfare is real, that the strongholds are real, that the imaginations and high things and thoughts in rebellion against God are real targets of the warfare, and that the warfare is conducted with weapons different from those of the world.

*Be sober, be vigilant; because your adversary the devil, as a roaring lion, walketh about, seeking whom he may devour* (1 Peter 5:8). Peter is naming the adversary as personal, active, hunting, and dangerous. The believer who is not sober and vigilant has misjudged the situation.

*Now the Spirit speaketh expressly, that in the latter times some shall depart from the faith, giving heed to seducing spirits, and doctrines of devils* (1 Timothy 4:1). Paul is naming that *seducing spirits* and *doctrines of devils* are real categories of spiritual operation, that they are at work in the latter times, and that the apostasy of believers from the faith is not merely intellectual drift but is being actively driven by spiritual agents.

The apostolic deposit's view of the situation is therefore unambiguous: the conflict in which Christians find themselves is a conflict with spiritual powers that operate behind and through human beings; those powers are real, hostile, active, and dangerous; the human beings through whom they operate are not the enemy but are the captured field on which the enemy operates; and the believer's task is to resist the spiritual powers, rescue the captured human beings, and remain sober and vigilant about the actual nature of what is happening.

This is not metaphor. This is the apostolic description of reality. The contemporary Christian temptation is to demythologize this language — to treat *principalities and powers* as quaint pre-modern shorthand for sociological forces, to treat *the devil* as a literary personification of human moral failure, to treat *seducing spirits* as a way of talking about cultural drift. The demythologization is itself one of the wiles by which the believer is disarmed. If the war is real, then a Christianity that has talked itself out of the war by translating its language into safer categories has surrendered the field before the battle began.

The Christos Civitas relational ethic is built on the apostolic description of reality, not on the demythologized version. The framework that follows assumes that the conversational partners and friends and family members and media personalities and political operatives we engage with are real human beings made in the image of God, and that the spirits operating through them — sometimes — are real spirits whose origin is not human. The work of the relational ethic is to distinguish the two with discipline, love the person, and resist the spirit. Soldier in a real war. Comrade fighting to rescue the captured. Enemy of the spiritual evil that has captured them.

## II. The Proper Direction of Hatred

Now to the harder claim. *Some things are to be hated.* The Christian who hates nothing has misread the moral universe.

*Ye that love the LORD, hate evil: he preserveth the souls of his saints; he delivereth them out of the hand of the wicked* (Psalm 97:10). David is not making a literary flourish. He is commanding the lovers of God to hate evil, on the grounds that this is what loving God involves.

*Do not I hate them, O LORD, that hate thee? and am not I grieved with those that rise up against thee? I hate them with perfect hatred: I count them mine enemies* (Psalm 139:21-22). David's hatred of those who hate God is not contradicted in the New Testament; it is *redirected* in the New Testament. The hatred is properly aimed at the spiritual reality that opposes God, not at the human beings caught up in opposing Him — but the hatred itself remains.

*Abhor that which is evil; cleave to that which is good* (Romans 12:9). Paul is commanding the abhorrence of evil. The Greek word *apostygountes* is the strongest available term for revulsion. The Christian who is indifferent to evil rather than abhorring it has failed at the most basic level of moral seriousness.

*Thou hatest all workers of iniquity. Thou shalt destroy them that speak leasing: the LORD will abhor the bloody and deceitful man* (Psalm 5:5-6). God Himself is named as hating, abhorring, destroying. The believer who imagines that love is the only attribute of God has read scripture selectively. God loves what is to be loved and hates what is to be hated, with both attitudes operating simultaneously and at full intensity.

*Through thy precepts I get understanding: therefore I hate every false way* (Psalm 119:104). The hatred of every false way is the consequence of understanding the precepts. To understand truth is to hate falsehood. The two are not separable.

So the Christian moral universe contains legitimate objects of hatred. *What is hated* is the evil itself, the principalities and powers, the spiritual wickedness, the false way, the iniquity, the seducing spirits, the doctrines of devils. *What is not hated* is the human being made in the image of God. The discipline is keeping the hatred and the love directed at the right objects.

This is what *hate the sin, love the sinner* means as a serious theological proposition rather than a sentimental cliché. It means that the believer, looking at a friend who has been captured by some destructive ideology — racial hatred, sexual confusion, ideological captivity to a political cult, spiritual deception about ultimate reality — is *required* to hate the thing that has captured the friend, and *required* to love the friend himself. The hatred of the capturing thing is the engine of rescue. The love of the captured friend is what the rescue is *for*. Without the hatred, there is no resistance to the evil and no will to rescue the friend from it. Without the love, the resistance to the evil becomes a war on the friend himself, which is exactly the corruption that contempt-culture conservatism and cancel-culture progressivism both embody in mirror image.

The contemporary cultural pressure is to flatten this distinction in two directions, depending on which culture-war side is doing the flattening:

**The progressive flattening** treats all hatred as illegitimate. *You should not hate anything; hatred is the problem; the failure to affirm what someone has chosen is itself a kind of hatred.* This is the move that has redefined orthodox Christianity itself as hate speech in much of contemporary discourse. It is theologically illiterate; the apostolic deposit explicitly commands the abhorrence of evil. The Christian who concedes this ground has surrendered the moral seriousness of the faith.

**The reactionary flattening** treats all hatred as legitimate when properly aimed at perceived enemies. *They are the enemy, and so they are the proper object of hatred; the cultural war is real and the people on the other side of it are the war.* This is the move that contempt-culture conservatism has been making for the last decade. It is theologically illiterate in mirror image; the apostolic deposit names the enemy as spiritual, not human, and the human beings through whom the spiritual enemy operates are precisely the captured ones who are to be rescued, not destroyed.

The Christian discipline is neither flattening. It is the simultaneous operation of both attitudes, properly directed: hatred toward the spiritual evil that captures, love toward the human being captured. Both at full intensity. Both at all times. The collapse of either one is a corruption of the other.

This is what *Component Two* of the Christos Civitas framework actually says — sharper than the first essay's version, more in line with what the apostolic deposit actually teaches. The believer is not commanded to be without hatred. The believer is commanded to hate the right things and love the right things, and to keep them straight even when the contemporary culture is pressuring him to collapse one into the other.

## III. The Proper Direction of Love — and the Phenomenology of God's Indwelling

Now the other side of the discipline. The love of the captured person is not optional kindness; it is theological necessity, and the warrant for it goes deeper than the first essay's articulation reached.

The phenomenology-of-the-Cross insight developed in the [April 25 essay](https://github.com/Renaissance-Ministries/RM/blob/main/Fellowship_essays/260425-phenomenology_of_the_cross_standalone.md) named that the Cross is morally coherent because the experiential recoil of God's nature against sin was absorbed by the only Being who could survive absorbing it. That insight, when extended to the broader question of God's relationship to His creatures, has direct relational-ethics consequences.

If God is omniscient — if His knowledge of His creatures is not the knowledge of a distant observer reading a report but the knowledge of a Being who experiences each person from the inside, who feels the world through every set of eyes, who tastes the cup of every soul — then the person captured by evil is the one through whom God Himself is bearing that evil. God is not at a distance from the friend's sin; God is feeling-it-from-the-inside as the friend feels it, and feeling also the recoil of His own holiness against what He is feeling. Sin is not just objectively wrong by some external metric. Sin is something God is experiencing through every soul who commits it, and the experience is grief, and the grief is real.

This is why the parable of the prodigal son (Luke 15) is what it is. The father in the parable is not the distant owner who has written off the wayward son. The father is the one who has been waiting, watching, looking down the road every day for the son's return — *and when he was yet a great way off, his father saw him, and had compassion, and ran, and fell on his neck, and kissed him* (Luke 15:20). The father has been bearing the son's absence as ongoing grief. The son's return ends the father's grief. The parable teaches that this is the structure of God's relationship with every prodigal — every captured human being whose captivity is being borne by God from inside the captivity itself.

If this is true, then correction of the captured person is not optional kindness. It is participation in God's labor of rescuing the person from being the one through whom God is bearing the evil. Listening to the captured friend, naming the captivity, calling the friend back from it, refusing to affirm the captivity as if it were freedom — all of this is part of what love actually is when love is directed at someone whose current state grieves God and to whom God is reaching with rescue. The friend who has been captured by a false ideology, a destructive sexual pattern, an ideological cult, a spiritual deception, is being grieved over by God right now, and our task is to participate in God's grief and God's rescue, not to manufacture a fictional peace by pretending the captivity is fine.

This sharpens what the first essay called *love the person more than you hold the position*. The position you hold is true; that is why you hold it. The person you love is captured; that is why you must not write them off. But the love of the person includes the willingness to grieve over their captivity *with God*, to name the captivity to them with the truth-telling that grief produces, and to refuse the false peace that affirms the captivity for the sake of the friendship. The first essay's *love the person more than you hold the position* could be read as *soften the position to keep the friendship*. That reading is wrong. The proper reading is *the position remains firm precisely because love of the person requires the position to be firm — the truth that the person needs to hear depends on the position being true and being held*.

A father who watches his son drift into addiction does not love the son more by affirming the addiction. He loves the son by telling the truth about the addiction, even when the telling costs him the son's affection in the short term. The truth-telling is the love. The pretended affirmation would be the abandonment. The same applies to every captivity: ideological, moral, spiritual, political. Love that is not willing to grieve over the captivity is not love; it is sentimentality posing as love. Love that grieves over the captivity *with God*, that names it honestly to the captured person, that keeps showing up over time in hope of rescue — that is what love actually is.

Component Two of the framework, properly stated, is therefore: *Hate the spiritual evil that captures; grieve with God over the person captured; remain in active rescue posture toward the captured person across whatever time the rescue requires*. The hatred is real and properly directed. The love is real and properly directed. The two operate simultaneously, and the combination is what the apostolic deposit calls *speaking the truth in love* (Ephesians 4:15).

## IV. Listening as Theological Discipline

The first essay treated listening as a virtue alongside firm conviction. That was right as far as it went, but it underweighted what listening actually is in the Christian moral economy. Listening is not just courtesy. Listening is theological discipline, required by two distinct warrants the apostolic deposit makes explicit.

**First warrant: listening pleases God to be known.** The person we are listening to is an image-bearer of God whose desire to be known by us reflects God's own desire to be known by His creatures. The whole structure of biblical revelation is God making Himself known: through creation (Romans 1:20), through Israel (Romans 9:4-5), through prophets (Hebrews 1:1), and finally through the Son (Hebrews 1:2). The pattern of revelation is the pattern of self-disclosure — a Being who wishes to be known, taking the initiative to make Himself known, in the hope that those to whom He discloses will receive the disclosure.

When we listen carefully to another person — actually listen, with attention, allowing their account of themselves to land before we respond — we are doing for our neighbor what God has done for us. We are receiving their self-disclosure with the kind of attention that makes the disclosure worth giving. The refusal to listen carefully is the refusal to do for the neighbor what God has done for us. *Be swift to hear, slow to speak, slow to wrath* (James 1:19) is not just prudential advice for managing arguments. It is a reflection of God's own posture toward His creatures, who hears their cries before they finish speaking and is patient through their full account.

There is also the converse: when we listen to another person, we please God by treating that person the way God treats them. *Inasmuch as ye have done it unto one of the least of these my brethren, ye have done it unto me* (Matthew 25:40). Listening carefully is one of the things we do to *the least of these* that we are doing to Christ Himself. The friend who sits across the table feeling unheard by us is feeling, in some real sense, unheard by us-as-bearers-of-Christ-toward-them. Our listening is part of the Christ we are bringing to the conversation.

**Second warrant: listening is the diagnostic discipline required for spirit-discernment.** *Beloved, believe not every spirit, but try the spirits whether they are of God: because many false prophets are gone out into the world* (1 John 4:1). The spirits operating in the conversation we are having must be tested. The testing requires the listening, because we cannot test what we have not heard. The friend who is speaking is bringing into the conversation some configuration of spirits — perhaps the Holy Spirit, perhaps a deceived enthusiasm for a partial truth, perhaps a captivity to something destructive, perhaps simple human exhaustion or grief that has nothing to do with spirits at all. We cannot know which without listening, and we cannot respond appropriately without knowing.

The four diagnostic possibilities the listener must keep open:

1. *The spirit being spoken is in agreement with the Holy Spirit and is to be affirmed.* When the friend says something true, recognize it as true and affirm it. The fellow-traveler may be more right than we are about a particular question. *Despise not prophesyings* (1 Thessalonians 5:20). The Spirit speaks through unexpected mouths, and the disciplined listener recognizes the Spirit's voice when it arrives.

2. *The spirit being spoken is absent, and the Holy Spirit is to be introduced.* When the friend is operating in a frame that does not yet contain the gospel, the listener's task is to introduce what is missing. This is the missionary task. It requires having listened first, because the introduction must be made to the actual frame the friend is in, not to a frame the listener has imagined.

3. *The spirit being spoken is present but inappropriate to the situation, and is to be redirected.* The friend is bringing real seriousness, real moral conviction, real spiritual energy — but it has been mis-channeled. Real concern for justice has been mis-channeled into ideological rage. Real concern for compassion has been mis-channeled into the affirmation of self-destructive patterns. Real concern for truth has been mis-channeled into conspiratorial cynicism. The listener who has heard carefully can name what has happened — *I see what you are responding to, and I share that concern; I think the response has gotten misdirected* — and offer a different channel for the same underlying energy.

4. *The spirit being spoken is opposed to the Holy Spirit and is to be resisted.* Some configurations are not misunderstanding to be corrected or partial truth to be supplemented; they are active opposition to God to be resisted. The seducing spirits and doctrines of devils Paul names in 1 Timothy 4:1 are real. The wolf in sheep's clothing of Matthew 7:15 is real. The disciplined listener, having heard carefully, can recognize when what is being brought into the conversation is not material to be corrected but a force to be resisted — not by attacking the person but by refusing to host the spirit.

The second warrant means listening is not optional in any responsible Christian engagement. The believer who responds before listening is responding to a phantom of the conversation rather than to the conversation itself, and his response will at best miss the mark and at worst do harm. The discipline is *swift to hear, slow to speak* — listening long enough to discern what spirit is operative, and only then bringing the response that spirit calls for.

This is Component Three of the framework: *Listen first, because listening pleases God to be known and is the diagnostic discipline required for spirit-discernment*.

## V. The Taxonomy of Certainties, Strong Inferences, and Prudential Judgments

Component Four of the framework is the taxonomy the first essay developed and that this essay carries forward without major change. Not all of our convictions occupy the same epistemic space, and the discipline of relational ethics requires keeping them sorted.

**Scriptural certainties** are confessed in the apostolic deposit, held firmly by the historic creedal traditions, and beyond reasonable Christian debate. The lordship of Jesus Christ. The Trinitarian God. The full deity and full humanity of Christ. The bodily resurrection. The authority of scripture. The reality of sin and the necessity of grace. The personhood of the unborn child from conception. The created order of marriage as the union of one man and one woman. The moral seriousness of work, property, and charity to the poor. These are not negotiable for orthodox Christians. They are confessed, and the confession is non-prudential.

**Strong scriptural inferences** are applications of clear scriptural principles where reasonable Christians have reached largely converging conclusions. The general pattern of constitutional ordering. The general moral seriousness of family life. The general pattern of fidelity in marriage and honesty in commerce. These are confidently held, with appropriate space for fellow Christians who emphasize different aspects of the same principles in different ways.

**Prudential judgments** are applications of broader principles to complex contemporary situations where reasonable Christians of good faith reach different conclusions. The optimal tax rate for funding the limited functions of government. The specific structure of immigration policy that best balances justice and order. The specific approach to public schooling. The specific economic policies. The specific foreign policy posture. The specific approach to environmental stewardship. Christians of good faith reach different conclusions on these questions, and the differences should be held with appropriate humility.

The discipline is keeping the categories straight. The cowardice of the soft Christian is to treat the certainties as prudential — to act as if the personhood of the unborn or the created order of marriage were matters of opinion on which reasonable Christians may differ. The rigidity of the hard Christian is the opposite — to treat the prudential as certainties, so that any Christian who disagrees on tax policy or specific cultural-moment tactics is recategorized as theologically suspect.

This component is not changed from the first essay, but its place in the overall framework is sharper. *On the certainties, do not negotiate, even at the cost of friendship* — and here the spiritual-warfare framing of Component One does its work. The certainties are non-negotiable not because we are dogmatic personality types but because they are the truths that constitute the gospel itself. To negotiate them is to negotiate the gospel, and the believer is not authorized to negotiate the gospel for any social benefit.

*On the prudential judgments, hold opinions but grant fellow Christians wide liberty to reach different conclusions, and refuse to use disagreement on prudential matters as a litmus test of fidelity* — and here the love-of-the-captured framing of Component Two does its work. The fellow Christian who reaches a different prudential conclusion than ours is not the enemy. The enemy is the spiritual evil that operates through the broader culture, and Christians who disagree on tactics in resisting that enemy should not be confused with the enemy itself. The contempt-culture failure to make this distinction is one of the spiritual evils Christian discourse currently suffers from.

## VI. The Four-Component Framework as a Whole

Stated together, the four components of the Christos Civitas relational ethic are:

**Component One: Hold the conviction firmly.** The conflict in which Christians find themselves is real spiritual warfare against principalities and powers. The convictions of the apostolic deposit are not opinions to be performed with appropriate uncertainty; they are the stand-line from which the warfare is conducted. *Stand therefore, having your loins girt about with truth, and having on the breastplate of righteousness* (Ephesians 6:14). The believer holds the conviction firmly because the conviction is the ground he is defending in a real war, not because he is temperamentally rigid.

**Component Two: Hate the evil; love the captured person.** The proper direction of hatred is toward the spiritual powers, the evil itself, the false way, the seducing spirits, the doctrines of devils. The proper direction of love is toward the human beings made in the image of God, including those captured by the evil and being grieved over by God in their captivity. The discipline is keeping the two directed at the right objects, simultaneously, at full intensity. The collapse of one into the other — contempt for the captured person, or sentimentality toward the captivity itself — is a corruption.

**Component Three: Listen first.** Listening pleases God to be known and is the diagnostic discipline required for spirit-discernment. The believer responding before listening is responding to a phantom of the conversation. The four diagnostic possibilities — affirm, introduce, redirect, resist — cannot be sorted without the listening, and the disciplined response cannot be made without the sorting.

**Component Four: Distinguish the certainties, the strong inferences, and the prudential judgments.** The discipline of keeping the categories straight is the discipline that distinguishes the soft Christian (who treats the certainties as prudential) from the rigid Christian (who treats the prudential as certainties). The mature Christian holds the certainties firmly, the strong inferences confidently, the prudential judgments charitably, and knows which is which in any given conversation.

The four components operate together. They do not compete with each other; they constrain each other. Component One without Component Two becomes the contempt-culture posture that has poisoned much of conservative Christian discourse. Component Two without Component One becomes the soft sentimentality that affirms captivities for the sake of friendship. Component Three without Components One and Two becomes the procedural neutrality that pretends to listen while having nothing to say. Component Four without the others becomes a rationalist sorting exercise that mistakes taxonomy for spiritual seriousness.

All four together constitute the relational ethic of the soldier of Christ who is engaged in real spiritual warfare, who hates what he should hate and loves what he should love and keeps each directed at the right objects, who listens carefully because listening is what he owes the image-bearer in front of him and is what the diagnostic task requires, and who knows the difference between a question on which the gospel itself rides and a question on which Christians of good faith may disagree.

This is the Christos Civitas Code of Relational Ethics. It is harder than either contempt or capitulation. It is the apostolic posture. It is what we are being called to.

## VII. Application to the Fellowship's Actual Situation

The framework is not abstract. It is alive in the conversations the fellowship is having right now.

**The conversation with Leonard.** The systematic Snuffer-engagement corpus committed earlier this week represents Component One done carefully. The convictions about the apostolic foreclosure of new restorations, the inadequacy of the framework Leonard has entered, and the structural problems of the Snuffer corpus are on the record. Component Two is the work that begins now. Leonard is not the Snuffer movement; Leonard is a brother captured by a framework that grieves God, and the work is to hate the framework while loving the brother. The grief is real. *I am unable to follow you into the framework you have entered* is Component One. *And I will keep showing up and keep listening and keep loving you across this disagreement* is Component Two. Component Three — listen first — is what every conversation with Leonard going forward requires; we cannot know what spirit is in the room with us until we have heard him out, and the response we make must be calibrated to what we have actually heard rather than to what we expected to hear. Component Four — knowing what is certainty and what is prudential — applies internally to our own posture; the certainty is the apostolic deposit, the prudential is how this specific friendship is held over what specific time, and the second is more open-ended than the first.

**The political and cultural conversations across family and friendship lines.** Every member of this fellowship has people on the other side of one or more cultural fault lines — the moral status of the unborn, the created order of marriage, the meaning and purpose of work, the legitimacy of constitutional ordering, the reality of moral truth. The four components apply across all of them. Component One: hold the convictions firmly because they are true. Component Two: hate the spiritual captivities — the ideological cults, the false anthropologies, the rebellions against created order — that have captured the friend or the family member, while loving the friend or family member fiercely as one whom God is grieving over. Component Three: listen carefully, long enough to discern which of the four diagnostic possibilities is operative in this particular conversation. Component Four: distinguish the questions on which the gospel itself rides from the prudential applications, and refuse to convert tactical disagreements with fellow believers into doctrinal anathemas.

**The cultural moment we are all living through.** America in 2026 is a place where spiritual warfare is operating openly through institutional channels — the redefinition of fundamental categories of human existence, the ideological capture of educational and corporate and governmental structures, the systematic production of contempt as the default mode of public discourse, the active resistance to the gospel in domains where the gospel was once welcomed. The fellowship is being called to witness to the truth in this moment, and the witnessing requires all four components. The conviction held firmly because the truth matters and the war is real. The hatred properly directed at the spiritual evil and the love properly directed at the human beings captured by it. The listening first, because the work is rescue and rescue requires understanding what has captured the captured. The taxonomy clearly held, because the moment is full of pressure to either soften the certainties or harden the prudential judgments, and both pressures must be resisted simultaneously.

This is what conscious participation in the Christos Civitas means in 2026. It is what the fellowship is being formed for. It is the work.

The Christian who has read the apostolic deposit carefully cannot avoid this language; it is the language the apostles themselves used. You should write it. I would read it.

What I would ask, as a brother in the broader fight against the corrosion of moral order in our country, is that the book not stop short of naming what your own observations are pointing at. The contempt-culture and the cancel-culture are not just bad social patterns; they are the surface manifestation of something deeper, and the something deeper has the structural shape of what the apostolic deposit calls principalities and powers. You may not need that vocabulary; the natural-law vocabulary may serve. But the diagnosis at depth is the same on either vocabulary, and the cure at depth is also the same: hold the truth firmly because the truth matters, hate the evil that captures, love the human beings who have been captured, listen carefully because listening is required, and know which of your positions are non-negotiable certainties and which are tactical applications where reasonable people may disagree.

That is the Christos Civitas Code of Relational Ethics, articulated in the language of the apostolic deposit. Translated into the language of natural-law conservatism, it would be different on the surface but the same at depth. Whichever vocabulary serves your readers best, the substance is what matters. Write the book. Name the patterns. Refuse to mirror the contempt. Hold the truth, hate the evil, love the captured, listen first, know which is which.

To the fellowship: this is the framework we are being called to live out. It is harder than either contempt or capitulation. It is the apostolic posture. It is what *speaking the truth in love* actually requires when the love is real and the truth is firm and the war is real and the captured friends are real and the rescue is the work we have been given to do.

Thomas
