# Denver Snuffer's Restoration

## A Christian Theological Evaluation of "Culture by Precept and Practice"

**Fellowship Discussion Essay | April 26, 2026**

**Source:** Lecture by Denver Snuffer, "Culture by Precept and Practice," delivered at a Covenant Christian conference, April 2026 (transcript provided to fellowship). Snuffer materials and conference recordings are accessible through [Covenant Christian Events](https://covenantchristianevents.com) and [Restoration Archives](https://restorationarchives.com).

**Context:** This essay engages Denver Snuffer because Leonard Hofheins, a member of our fellowship, identified Snuffer as the present teacher whose movement Leonard has joined. Yesterday's [Three Conversations at One Table](https://github.com/Renaissance-Ministries/RM/blob/main/Fellowship_essays/260426-three_conversations_at_one_table.md) flagged Snuffer's claim to face-to-face encounters with Christ as the question requiring careful engagement. This essay is that engagement. It is written in the spirit of Susan's procedural counsel: examine the *root* of a movement before adjudicating its branches.

---

To the Fellowship —

I have now read Denver Snuffer's recent conference lecture in full, and I want to share my evaluation honestly. Snuffer is a serious man — a learned student of Mormon history, an able rhetorician, a sincere believer, and a former practicing attorney who has plainly thought hard about what he claims and stands behind. He is also clearly loved by those in his movement, and his pastoral instincts toward them are genuine. Anyone engaging him should engage him at his level.

That is what I am going to try to do. I will name what is honorable, identify where his critique of Salt Lake LDS converges with critiques outside observers would make, and then walk through the load-bearing claims of his theology and ask whether they hold up under the test of historic biblical Christianity. I will not pretend that the differences are denominational. They are not. But I will not sneer, and I will not flatten the man into a caricature.

Leonard, this is for you, and it is for the rest of the fellowship who are now thinking about whether the Snuffer movement is what it claims to be.

---

## I. What Is Honorable in Snuffer

Before I say anything critical, let me say what is true. Several things in Snuffer's lecture deserve genuine respect.

**1. He is honest about his own history.** Snuffer was a Latter-day Saint for 40 years. He served on a stake high council, taught gospel doctrine, taught at BYU Education Week, taught Institute at the University of Utah law school. He did not leave Salt Lake LDS in pique or rebellion. He left because, as he tells it, the institution forced him to choose between the book he had written to *preserve* faith in the Restoration (*Passing the Heavenly Gift*) and his membership. He chose the book. He was excommunicated on the 40-year anniversary of his baptism, and he describes that day with notable composure. Whatever else is true, this is not the testimony of a casual dissident.

**2. His critique of Salt Lake LDS is internally substantive.** Some of what he says about the modern Salt Lake institution is precisely what a careful outside observer would say. He documents the corporate concentration of wealth (he mentions the Ensign Peak portfolio), the alteration of the temple endowment over generations, the recent prohibition by Russell Nelson against using the word "Mormon" in conjunction with the Mormon legacy, the lawsuit by the Salt Lake corporation against John Dehlin for using "Mormon" in his podcast title. He pulls no punches in describing what he sees as institutional decline. As I noted in yesterday's essay, the convergence of internal Mormon dissent and external Christian critique on these specific points is worth naming. Snuffer is not making these critiques up. They are observable.

**3. He honors Joseph Smith as a man.** Snuffer's portrait of Smith — *not* the polygamist that the modern LDS Church now (he says) defames in its essays, but a man who actively opposed polygamy, brought disciplinary proceedings against those who practiced it, and was killed before he could bring Brigham Young to a high council — is a particular reading of the historical record. I will return to whether that reading is sustainable. But what is honorable in it is the unwillingness to join the cynical chorus that has reduced Smith to a sexual predator. Whatever Smith was, he was a complex human being who bore enormous costs for what he believed, and Snuffer's refusal to flatten him into the caricature is dignified.

**4. He cares about scripture.** Snuffer's movement has produced, over years, a "Restoration Edition" of scripture: the Joseph Smith Translation of the Old Testament (Volume 1), the Joseph Smith Translation of the New Testament plus a revised Book of Mormon plus a glossary of gospel terms (Volume 2), and the Lectures on Faith plus revised Doctrine and Covenants plus a modern-English Book of Mormon called "The Covenant of Christ" (Volume 3). The work is serious. The textual scholarship — comparing the printer's manuscript of the Book of Mormon with later editions, restoring John Gilbert's punctuation choices, identifying what Snuffer calls "revelation creep" in the 1835 Doctrine and Covenants — is real scholarship. Whether the *premise* of the project is sound is what I will examine. But the project is not lazy.

**5. His pastoral instincts are real.** The story he tells about Sophia, the profoundly handicapped child of one of his community members, is not boilerplate. He sees her not as a burden to her parents but as a *gift to the entire community* — and he names plainly that if the community really understood what God was up to, the burden would be borne in common. That is the Christian instinct. *"Bear ye one another's burdens, and so fulfil the law of Christ"* (Galatians 6:2). I commend it.

**6. His exhortation to the young is largely sound.** His advice to young people — pursue practical skills, do not let AI think for you, develop the discipline of comprehending rather than merely accepting answers, marry, have children, expect that your role is to stand in the gap when the existing order fails — is, in its substance, Christian. I would not say it differently. The cultural diagnosis (a generation that cannot do basic arithmetic, that defers thinking to algorithms, that has lost the discipline of reading and writing in cursive) is not exaggerated.

**7. He warns against AI as a substitute for thinking.** Snuffer is right that AI gives answers without regard to truth, that the answer machine will satisfy you with falsehood as readily as with truth, and that the discipline of independent comprehension cannot be outsourced. He is also right that lawyers have been disbarred for citing AI-generated fake cases in briefs. As someone working extensively with AI tools myself, I share his concern. AI is a useful editor; it is not an oracle. Use it as such.

These seven things should be said before any criticism is made. They are real, and Snuffer earns them.

## II. The Lecture's Most Striking Feature — What Is Almost Absent

Now to the harder material. And the first thing I want to name is not a doctrinal error but an *absence*.

Snuffer's lecture runs roughly 90 minutes. It is titled "Culture by Precept and Practice" and is delivered to a community he describes as "Covenant Christians." In those 90 minutes, the topics he develops at length are:

- The pattern of patriarchal blessings from Adam through Lehi
- The procedure for adding names to the recorder's book at baptism
- The voting procedure for setting Keith Henderson apart as the movement's patriarch
- The textual history of the Restoration scriptures and the production of the Restoration Edition
- The corruption of Salt Lake LDS scripture and authority claims
- The biographical account of his excommunication
- The defense of Joseph Smith as the founding prophet
- The eschatology of a "modest, hardly a ripple" fulfillment of end-times prophecy
- Pastoral advice to young people
- A closing testimony

What the lecture spends almost no time on:

- **The person and work of Jesus Christ in the historic Christian sense.** The Cross is mentioned in passing. The resurrection is mentioned briefly. The atonement, in any developed sense, is not addressed.
- **Justification by faith, the imputed righteousness of Christ, regeneration, sanctification, union with Christ.** None of these classical soteriological categories appears.
- **The gospel as God's solution to human sin.** Sin is mentioned, but as a moral failing within the Restoration community ("repent of accusing"), not as the cosmic dilemma the Cross resolves.
- **The Holy Spirit as the regenerating presence indwelling the believer.** The Spirit is implicit but not developed.
- **Trinitarian theology in any classical sense.** The Father, Son, and Spirit are mentioned, but the relations are not developed, and Snuffer specifically (and approvingly) notes that his Restoration Book of Mormon has been edited to *eliminate* what he characterizes as Trinitarian misreadings introduced by John Gilbert's 1830 punctuation choices in the original printer's manuscript. The substance of Trinitarian orthodoxy is, in the Restoration framework, treated as a problem to be removed rather than a truth to be preserved.

This is striking. A 90-minute lecture from a teacher whose community calls itself *Christian* should center on Christ — on what He is, what He did, what He accomplished, and what life in Him consists of. Snuffer's lecture centers, instead, on *the Restoration* — on the recovery of an ancient pattern of family-government, the production of accurate scripture, the establishment of priestly offices through revealed direction, and the vindication of Joseph Smith as the prophet through whom the recovery began.

I want to put this charitably: Snuffer is delivering a *conference lecture* at a movement gathering, not a sermon on the gospel. He may well have other lectures in which Christ and the Cross are central. The 10 talks he gave at the time of his excommunication may carry that weight. I have not heard those.

But I have heard *this* one. And in this one, the load-bearing claims are about the *Restoration*, not about Christ. That observation is theologically diagnostic. Movements whose center of gravity is the recovery of ancient patterns and authoritative offices — rather than the finished work of Christ on the Cross and the present work of the Spirit applying that finished work — are, structurally, restorationist movements. They place the locus of redemptive activity in the recovery of authority and scripture rather than in the once-for-all event at Calvary. That is the *family resemblance* the movement bears, regardless of how often the name of Christ is mentioned within it.

The question we must keep asking is not "does Snuffer mention Christ?" but "what does Snuffer say Christ *did*, and what does the believer's standing in Christ *consist of*?" Until those questions are answered, the family resemblance to historic Christianity is unsettled.

## III. The Five Load-Bearing Claims

Setting aside what is admirable and what is absent, let me address the five doctrinal claims on which Snuffer's movement actually rests, and ask whether each holds up under biblical Christianity.

### 1. The reopened canon

Snuffer states unambiguously: *"We have the fullness of the scriptures only us period."* The Restoration Edition includes the Joseph Smith Translation of the Bible (recovered from the RLDS line in its purer form), a revised Book of Mormon (in his account, the most accurate version available), the Lectures on Faith (re-canonized by community vote), the original Doctrine and Covenants minus what he calls inflation through subsequent committee editing, and a modern-English Book of Mormon called *The Covenant of Christ*. He concludes the section by declaring that, after years of effort, he is now in a position to inform the world that it has been warned.

This is the load-bearing structural claim of the entire movement. Without an open canon and without authoritative scripture beyond the Bible, the movement has no distinctive ground to stand on. With an open canon, everything else follows.

The historic Christian position is that the canon is closed. Not because no further truth could ever be revealed, but because the apostolic deposit is sufficient. *"All scripture is given by inspiration of God, and is profitable for doctrine, for reproof, for correction, for instruction in righteousness: that the man of God may be perfect, throughly furnished unto all good works"* (2 Timothy 3:16-17). Paul's claim is that the inspired writings *thoroughly furnish* the man of God — they leave nothing essential out. *"The faith which was once delivered unto the saints"* (Jude 1:3) was delivered *once*. There is no "yet to be re-delivered."

Snuffer's reply, implicit in the lecture, is that the once-delivered faith *was* corrupted through textual transmission, doctrinal accretion, and institutional self-interest, and therefore needs restoration. This is also the LDS position. It is the load-bearing claim of every Restoration movement. And the test it must pass is the same test the original Christianity-vs-Mormonism question raises: *did Christ keep His promise?*

> *"The gates of hell shall not prevail against it."* — Matthew 16:18

If Christ kept that promise, then the Holy Spirit preserved a sufficient witness to the gospel through the centuries between Pentecost and Palmyra. The restorationist claim — that no such witness was preserved, that the canon needs to be reopened, that 19th-century American prophets were necessary to recover what 1,800 years of faithful believers had lost — is structurally identical whether it comes from Salt Lake LDS or from Snuffer's Covenant Christians. And it is structurally incompatible with the apostolic foreclosure:

> *"But though we, or an angel from heaven, preach any other gospel unto you than that which we have preached unto you, let him be accursed."* — Galatians 1:8

Paul did not say "any other gospel until a corrected scripture comes along." He said *any other*. The foreclosure is unconditional, and it pre-dates by 1,800 years anything Joseph Smith or Denver Snuffer could possibly bring forward.

This is not a small disagreement. This is the load-bearing question. If Snuffer is right that the canon is open and his Restoration Edition is uniquely the fullness of scripture, then historic Christianity has been fundamentally insufficient for two millennia. If Paul is right that the gospel was delivered once and an angel from heaven cannot add to it, then the entire Restoration project — Smith's, Snuffer's, and every variant in between — is structurally impossible. Both cannot be true.

### 2. Continuing prophetic and priestly authority

Snuffer does not merely *teach* in his community. He *acts* with prophetic authority. In the lecture he describes:

- **Receiving identification of Keith Henderson from the Lord** as the man to be set apart as the movement's patriarch. The Lord identified him "earlier this week"; Snuffer set him apart on Thursday; the community ratified the appointment by mutual unanimous vote on the day of the conference.
- **Receiving direct revelation about scripture-production**, with the Lord's authorial involvement in producing the Restoration Edition.
- **Receiving inspired prayers** that are then canonized as Section 156 of the new Teachings and Commandments.
- **Speaking with the authority to declare** that the world has now been warned and is therefore accountable.

The historic Christian position on this is delicate but firm. Christians do not deny that God can and does communicate with His people through the Spirit, through providence, through the conviction of conscience, and through the application of Scripture to particular circumstances. The Holy Spirit speaks to every believer who will listen. *"As many as are led by the Spirit of God, they are the sons of God"* (Romans 8:14).

But there is a categorical difference between *Spirit-led discernment by ordinary believers* and *prophetic authority in the apostolic sense* — the kind of authority by which Moses spoke for the Lord and by which Paul wrote scripture. The latter category, biblically, requires submission of the prophetic claim to the existing apostolic deposit. Paul submitted his Damascus-road revelation to the apostles in Jerusalem (Galatians 1:18 - 2:10) precisely because new prophetic claims must be verified against what was already delivered. The Bereans were commended for searching the Scriptures daily *to verify whether what Paul preached was so* (Acts 17:11). This is the standard biblical procedure for any prophetic claim: verify by the existing Scripture.

The standard does not bend for Joseph Smith, and it does not bend for Denver Snuffer. The question to be put to Snuffer's prophetic claims is: *do they square with the apostolic deposit?* If they do — then Snuffer is at most a teacher of what we already have, useful but not adding to canon. If they don't — then Galatians 1:8 forecloses them.

The specific instance of the Henderson appointment is illustrative. Snuffer says the Lord identified Henderson to him; the community then ratified by mutual unanimous vote. This is the procedure of a movement *claiming the same kind of authority the apostles had*, with no submission to a higher authority outside the movement itself. There is no Jerusalem council. There is no apostolic deposit by which the claim can be tested. The claim is self-authenticating: the Lord told Snuffer; Snuffer told the community; the community said yes. By that procedure, *any* claim to direct divine guidance could be ratified. The procedure has no built-in test for falsity.

This is the structural problem with continuing prophetic authority outside the apostolic deposit: it cannot be checked. And what cannot be checked, will eventually be exploited — not necessarily by Snuffer, who appears sincere, but by his successors, or by any movement that adopts the same procedural pattern. This is precisely why the early church closed the canon and submitted all subsequent teaching to it.

### 3. The face-to-face Christophany claim

Leonard reported in fellowship that Snuffer claims face-to-face encounters with Christ comparable to Moses on Sinai or Paul on the Damascus road. The lecture itself does not explicitly make this claim in those words, though it includes multiple references to direct guidance Snuffer has received from the Lord — including the identification of Keith Henderson as the man to be set apart as the movement's patriarch, and the description of the Restoration Edition's production as accomplished with the Lord's direct involvement.

I want to engage this with the seriousness it deserves rather than dismissing it.

Christian history does include genuine instances of direct divine encounter — Paul on the Damascus road most clearly, John on Patmos, and a tradition of mystical encounter within the broader church (Teresa of Avila, Julian of Norwich, John Bunyan's experiences, John Wesley's heart-warming at Aldersgate). The category is real.

But the biblical test for any such claim is consistent and unsentimental: the test is the *content* of what was received and whether it accords with the apostolic deposit. *"To the law and to the testimony: if they speak not according to this word, it is because there is no light in them"* (Isaiah 8:20). Paul gave this principle to his own Galatian converts: *if I myself, or an angel from heaven, preach a different gospel from what I first preached, let me be accursed* (Galatians 1:8). Paul made *himself* subject to the test.

So the question to Snuffer is the same question Paul put to himself: does the *content* of what you have received square with the gospel the apostles delivered? And here the answer must be drawn from the lecture itself.

In Snuffer's account of what the Lord is currently doing, the work consists of: producing accurate scripture, establishing patriarchal blessings, recording names of the baptized, identifying men for priestly offices, and preparing a community to persevere through what he describes as a modest, almost-undetectable fulfillment of end-times prophecy. *Christ crucified for sinners* is not the substance of what Snuffer reports the Lord to be saying through him. The Restoration is.

That is the diagnostic. A genuine prophetic word from the risen Christ would foreground the very thing Christ Himself foregrounded: *"And he expounded unto them in all the scriptures the things concerning himself"* (Luke 24:27). When Christ instructed Cleopas and his companion on the Emmaus road, the substance was *the Christ Himself, suffering and entering into His glory*. When the Spirit testifies, He testifies *of Christ* (John 15:26). The Spirit's witness is unmistakably and centrally Christological.

A movement whose claimed prophetic word centers on the Restoration rather than on Christ may be reporting a real experience of *something*, but the test of whether that something is the Christ of the apostolic deposit is whether the report magnifies *Him* as Scripture magnifies Him. The lecture's evidence is mixed at best, and the absence I noted in Section II is the diagnostic absence.

### 4. The eschatology of the modest restoration

Snuffer's eschatology is one of the more distinctive features of the lecture, and it deserves careful naming.

His position is that the events leading up to the Lord's second coming are unfolding *now*, in modest and almost-undetectable form, through his movement. The pattern, he argues, is the same as the first coming: Christ came as one barely noticed by his contemporaries — recognized by only a small remnant. Likewise, the second coming will be *prepared for* by a remnant whose work is invisible to the world. The prophecies, he asserts, *require* that the overwhelming majority never figure out what is happening — and those who want to understand the prophetic fulfillment leading up to the Lord's return must pay attention to what his movement is currently doing.

This is a high-stakes eschatological claim. Snuffer is saying that *his movement* is the mechanism by which the second-coming prophecies are being fulfilled — and that the rest of mankind, including the bulk of the visible church, will be reduced to ashes under the feet of those who are prepared for the Lord's return.

The biblical witness about the Lord's return is in significant tension with this account. Christ's return is described, repeatedly, as universally visible and unmistakable:

> *"For as the lightning cometh out of the east, and shineth even unto the west; so shall also the coming of the Son of man be."* — Matthew 24:27

> *"Behold, he cometh with clouds; and every eye shall see him, and they also which pierced him."* — Revelation 1:7

> *"The Lord himself shall descend from heaven with a shout, with the voice of the archangel, and with the trump of God."* — 1 Thessalonians 4:16

The biblical pattern is not "modest, hardly a ripple, fulfilled invisibly through one remnant movement." The biblical pattern is *modest first coming, unmistakable second coming*. Christ's first appearance was hidden because the redemption had to be accomplished through a substitutionary death; His second appearance will be open because the redemption is being applied through universal judgment and resurrection. To analogize the second to the first by way of "modest fulfillment in our movement" is to misread the structural pattern of the two comings.

There is also a pastoral and ethical concern. When a teacher tells his community that *they* are the ones through whom prophecy is being fulfilled, and that those outside the community will become *ashes under their feet*, the rhetoric trends toward apocalyptic in-group identity formation. The community becomes *the* people of God in a way that walls them off from the broader body of Christ. This is the dynamic that has produced every sectarian Restoration movement from the Millerites forward, and it is the dynamic that Joseph Smith's own movement instantiated. Snuffer, who I do not doubt is sincere, may not see his movement going where Salt Lake LDS went. But the structural dynamic is the same, and the structural dynamic is what produces, over generations, the kind of in-group certainty that becomes its own form of authoritarianism.

### 5. The Christology that is not articulated

I have saved the most consequential observation for last, because it is the most important.

In the entire lecture, Snuffer does not articulate a Christology that distinguishes the Christ he is teaching from the Christ of historic Christianity. He uses the name Jesus Christ. He testifies of the resurrection. He affirms that Jesus lives and speaks. But he does not say:

- Whether the Son is eternally God or whether (as in classical LDS Christology) He progressed to divinity
- Whether the Son is consubstantial with the Father (Nicene homoousios) or whether (as in classical LDS Christology) He is a separate divine being subordinate to the Father
- Whether the Son took on flesh in a virgin birth in the historically Christian sense or whether (as in the more elaborated strands of Mormon Christology) He was conceived through some form of heavenly-Father-and-Mary union
- Whether the Cross is the central event of redemption (as in historic Christianity) or whether (as in classical LDS theology) the atonement was primarily accomplished in Gethsemane

The reason this matters is that Snuffer's movement *appropriates* the name "Christian" without specifying which Christ. The community calls itself Covenant Christians. The modern-English Book of Mormon is called The Covenant of Christ. The lecture closes with "Jesus Christ lives, speaks, is engaged." But the *content* of who that Christ is is not given. The hearer is left to assume that Snuffer means the historic Christ — the eternal Word, consubstantial with the Father, born of a virgin, crucified for sinners, raised on the third day, ascended, reigning, returning. But because Snuffer is operating from within a Mormon-derived framework that has historically articulated a different Christology, the assumption may be unfounded.

What I would press Snuffer on, if I had the chance, is exactly this: *which Christ?* Tell me about His pre-existence. Tell me about His conception. Tell me about His relation to the Father. Tell me what His death accomplished, and what union with Him by faith consists of. Tell me whether He is the eternal *I AM* or a being who progressed to divinity. *Until those questions are answered with Christological precision, the movement's claim to be Christian is unverified.*

This is not a hostile question. It is the most important question. And it is the question that, in 90 minutes of "Culture by Precept and Practice," is not actually answered.

## IV. A Practical Test — What Would the Apostles Say?

Let me put one practical test to the whole framework and then close with the pastoral implication for Leonard and for the fellowship.

Imagine the apostle Paul walking into Snuffer's conference. He hears the testimonies. He observes the vote. He receives a copy of the Restoration Edition. He listens to the lecture in full.

What does Paul say?

I think Paul says, gently but firmly, the things he said in his letters. He says: *let no man take your crown* (Revelation 3:11) — beware of additions to what you have received. He says: *though we, or an angel from heaven, preach any other gospel unto you than that which we have preached unto you, let him be accursed* (Galatians 1:8). He says: *I have planted, Apollos watered; but God gave the increase* (1 Corinthians 3:6) — the work is the gospel of Christ crucified, not the recovery of priestly offices. He says: *we preach Christ crucified, unto the Jews a stumblingblock, and unto the Greeks foolishness; but unto them which are called, both Jews and Greeks, Christ the power of God, and the wisdom of God* (1 Corinthians 1:23-24).

Paul would not say Snuffer is insincere. He would say Snuffer is *sincere about the wrong thing*. The center of the apostolic gospel is not the Restoration. The center of the apostolic gospel is Christ crucified, risen, ascended, indwelling His people by the Spirit, returning in glory. A movement whose center is the Restoration — however eloquent, however well-organized, however sincere — has shifted the center of gravity. And in spiritual matters, the center of gravity is everything.

## V. What I Say to Leonard, and to the Fellowship

Leonard, you know I love you, and you know this conversation is one we have been circling for months. Let me say plainly what I take this lecture to mean.

**Snuffer is a serious man who is teaching a serious framework, and the framework is not the historic Christian faith.** It is a refined variant of the Restoration tradition that began with Joseph Smith. It shares with Salt Lake LDS the load-bearing claim that *Christianity-from-the-apostles-onward was insufficient* and required restoration. It shares with Salt Lake LDS the structural pattern of *continuing prophetic authority outside the closed canon*. It diverges from Salt Lake LDS in important ways — rejecting polygamy, rejecting the corporate church, rejecting the institutional power-concentration that Snuffer has rightly named — but the *root* is the same Restoration claim.

The question Susan put to us yesterday is the question that still needs to be answered. Before adjudicating any branch of the Restoration tree, *examine the root*. The root is Joseph Smith's first vision and the claim that the church Christ founded had so completely failed that a new prophetic restoration was required. If the root is sound, every Restoration movement — including Snuffer's — can in principle be evaluated on its own merits. If the root is not sound, every Restoration movement falls with it, however refined the branches.

I want to be honest with you about where I stand. I do not believe the root is sound. I believe Christ kept His promise that the gates of hell would not prevail against His church. I believe the apostolic deposit is sufficient. I believe the canon is closed. I believe the gospel is centered on Christ crucified and risen, applied to believers by the Spirit, with a return that will be unmistakable rather than modest. And I believe — with all the love sixty years of friendship can hold — that the search for ongoing prophetic authority outside the closed canon is, in the end, a search for a Christ other than the Christ the apostles preached.

That does not mean Snuffer is a charlatan. He is not. That does not mean his community produces no good fruit. They appear, by Snuffer's own description, to produce real fruit — communities of mutual care, families that endure, sincere disciples who study and pray. Real things are happening among them, and the Spirit may be at work despite the framework rather than because of it, just as the Spirit works among Salt Lake LDS, among Catholics, and among compromised Protestants, *despite* the doctrinal errors of each.

But for those of us who are watching from outside, the framework matters. And the framework Snuffer is teaching is not the framework of the apostolic gospel. It is the framework of nineteenth-century American restorationism, refined and re-edited but structurally continuous with what it has always been.

I commend to you, Leonard, the same Christ I commended in yesterday's essay — the eternal Christ who paid the actual debt by bearing the actual response of God's nature against actual sin in actual flesh on an actual Cross on an actual day in history. Not a Christ requiring restoration to be reached. Not a Christ approached through priestly offices set apart by votes of mutual agreement. Not a Christ veiled behind a movement claiming to be the modest fulfillment of end-times prophecy. The Christ of the apostolic deposit. The Christ of John 1:1. The Christ of Hebrews 13:8. The Christ who *is* the same yesterday, today, and forever — and who has not, since Pentecost, lacked a faithful witness on the earth.

To the fellowship: this is the work Snuffer's movement requires of us. Not dismissal. Not sneering. Not the assumption that our Mormon-derived neighbors are not sincere or are not seeking. *Genuine engagement with the framework, on its actual claims, by the actual standard of the apostolic deposit.* And then the willing presentation of the actual Christ to those who are seeking Him, in whatever movement they currently find themselves.

We commend Christ. We honor the seekers. We do not soften the framework. And we trust the Holy Spirit to do, in His time, the work that argument alone cannot do — the work of bringing every honest seeker to the Christ they have been searching for, whether or not they have yet recognized Him by name.

Thomas
