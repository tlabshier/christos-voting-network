# Eight Strongholds: A Christos Civitas Reading of the "Ingredients" Joan Swirsky Inventoried

**Fellowship Discussion Essay | April 30, 2026**

**Occasion:** A column by Joan Swirsky published today at NewsWithViews uses a Food Network metaphor as scaffolding for an inventory of eight characteristics she attributes to Democrats as a class — *consistent negativity, dependence on big government, behaving like infants/children, anger on parade, jealousy, victims R us, conceit, intolerance.* The column is sharp, polemical, and aimed at producing the reader's confidence that the November 2026 midterms will properly dispose of *all the Democrat bums.* I want to engage the column not because Joan and I disagree about most of the substantive concerns she names — we do not, on most of them — but because the piece is a published example of a posture the [Christos Civitas Code](https://github.com/Renaissance-Ministries/RM/tree/main/CFE_christos_fellowship_essays/book/christos_civitas_code) just diagnosed as one of the corruptions of contemporary conservative discourse, and because the eight items Joan inventoried turn out, on closer inspection, to be a remarkably accurate catalog of *demonic strongholds* once they are detached from their misattribution to a single political tribe. This essay re-purposes Joan's inventory toward what it actually catalogs.

**The philosophical position governing this essay:** The strongholds catalogued in what follows are cross-tribal. They capture human beings on every side of every fault line — left and right, secular and religious, educated and uneducated, native-born and immigrant — and the Christian who imagines they capture only the political opposition has misread the spiritual situation. The framework therefore applies symmetrically to all human beings caught up in any of these captivities. *And* — this is the institutional-accountability extension that this essay introduces alongside the cross-tribal framing — to the extent that any organization, party, media outlet, or movement systematically appeals to people captured by these spirits, mobilizes its coalition by activating these strongholds, and rewards the behaviors that nurture the captivities, that organization is functioning as a vehicle for those spirits regardless of whether any individual member intends this. Diagnosis at the institutional level is necessary because exclusive focus on individuals risks a false symmetry that obscures real differences in how organizations relate to particular strongholds. The framework therefore operates at three levels: spiritual (the principalities and powers themselves), individual (the human persons captured), and institutional (the organizations that function as vehicles). The first two are the ground of love and rescue. The third is the ground of structural critique. All three are required for honest political and cultural theology.

**Context:** This is the second essay to work *from* the four-component Christos Civitas Code framework rather than developing it, following the [Leaman political-violence engagement](https://github.com/Renaissance-Ministries/RM/blob/main/CFE_christos_fellowship_essays/essays/260429-political_violence_leaman_redirect.md). Where the Leaman essay applied the *redirect* move to a sharp data-driven post on the political-violence question, this essay applies the same move to a polemical piece by a fellow conservative and develops a stronghold-by-stronghold inventory of cross-tribal demonic captivities as its substantive contribution.

---

To the Fellowship —

Joan Swirsky has done us a small favor. I do not think she meant to, but the substantive accuracy of what she observed is greater than the rhetorical use she put it to, and the inventory she catalogued — once detached from its tribal misattribution — turns out to be a useful theological resource. Joan named eight characteristic traits she sees in the contemporary political moment. She labeled them *Democrat ingredients.* They are not. They are demonic strongholds, eight of them, each with its own operating signature, each capturing human beings across every political and cultural line, each reliably producing the same shape of damage in the lives of its captives wherever it captures them. The political party that systematically activates a stronghold and mobilizes its coalition through that stronghold is doing the spirit's organizational work, and that institutional question deserves its own honest naming. But the strongholds themselves are cross-tribal, and the believer who confuses *the spirit that captures* with *the political opposition that the spirit has captured* has misjudged who his enemy is.

Paul named the situation precisely. *We wrestle not against flesh and blood, but against principalities, against powers, against the rulers of the darkness of this world, against spiritual wickedness in high places* (Ephesians 6:12). The wrestling is not with our progressive neighbors. The wrestling is not with our family members who voted differently. The wrestling is not with the eighteen-year-olds in the polling data who told YouGov they think political violence is sometimes justified. The wrestling is with what has *captured* them. The captured are not the enemy. The spirits that captured them are. And the same spirits, with equal reliability, capture us — in different ways, through different vehicles, on different questions, but with the same fundamental dynamic of substituting themselves for the real goods we were reaching for.

So let us walk through Joan's inventory. Not to defend Democrats — many of the patterns Joan observed in contemporary Democratic Party rhetoric and policy are genuinely there. But to relocate the diagnosis at the level the apostolic deposit places it. The eight strongholds. Each named, briefly, with its biblical resonance, its operating signature, the captivity it produces, and the institutional patterns through which it currently operates. Where the institutional patterns implicate one party more visibly than the other, that will be said. Where the patterns operate on every side, that will also be said. The framework is not partisan. It is principled. The same eyes that see the strongholds in our neighbors are required to see them in ourselves.

A note before we begin. There is a pastoral instinct that needs to be active throughout this work. The eight strongholds are not just abstract spiritual categories. They are the actual operating modes by which actual human beings — our daughter, our brother-in-law, our college roommate, our former pastor — are being held. When I name a stronghold and describe its operating signature, I am describing what is currently happening inside someone we love. The naming is not contempt. It is diagnosis, in the medical sense, in service of rescue. *Speaking the truth in love, may grow up into him in all things, which is the head, even Christ* (Ephesians 4:15). The truth-telling is the love. And — this is the discipline the [eight-strongholds essay sits inside](https://github.com/Renaissance-Ministries/RM/blob/main/CFE_christos_fellowship_essays/book/christos_civitas_code/ch05_component_two_hate_evil_love_captured.md) — the people Joan was speaking to, when she wrote her column, are the same people God experiences His own neighborly love through, from inside. The pastoral target of this essay is therefore not Joan's audience of fellow conservatives but God-in-the-captured-neighbors who are reading this and need correction delivered as God would want it delivered. Humor is allowed where humor is appropriate; the humor is directed at the spirits, not at the captured.

## Stronghold I — Negativity

Joan's first item is *consistent negativity*: she describes a settled posture in which the sky is always falling, the glass is always half empty, and the worst is always right around the corner.

This is the spirit of perpetual lament, and it has biblical names. *Murmuring* and *complaining* are how the apostles named it in their Greek (*gongusmos*, the murmuring of the children of Israel in the wilderness; *me gogguzete*, *do not murmur*, in 1 Corinthians 10:10). It is the captivity that produces in human beings an inability to see the good in what God has given. *Whatsoever things are true, whatsoever things are honest, whatsoever things are just, whatsoever things are pure, whatsoever things are lovely, whatsoever things are of good report; if there be any virtue, and if there be any praise, think on these things* (Philippians 4:8). The spirit of negativity is the spirit that systematically refuses Philippians 4:8 — that finds, in every situation, the angle from which the situation looks dark, and absorbs the human being into a permanent posture of grievance.

The captivity captures human beings on every side. The progressive captive of negativity sees the United States as fundamentally a story of injustice and oppression, with whatever progress has been made overwhelmed by the structures that remain. The conservative captive of negativity sees America as already lost, the culture beyond saving, every passing day producing more evidence that the country is past the point of recovery. Both are captured by the same spirit. The signature is the same: an organizing posture of *the worst is right around the corner*, an inability to receive any good news without immediate qualification, a settled assumption that the universe is bent against what we love.

The institutional vehicles for this stronghold are real. Cable news on every side has built a business model on activating it. The progressive captives are reliably nourished by one set of channels; the conservative captives by another. The Democratic Party has, in its rhetorical strategy, made systematic appeals to the negativity-captured progressive — *the country is in a state of crisis, fascism is at the door, the planet is dying, all is lost without our urgent action.* The Republican Party has, in its rhetorical strategy, made systematic appeals to the negativity-captured conservative — *the culture is collapsing, the institutions are gone, all is lost without our urgent action.* Both parties are mobilizing the stronghold's captives. Both parties are reliable vehicles for this spirit. The believer who recognizes the stronghold sees the operation on both sides simultaneously, refuses to be activated by either, and grieves over the captured on both sides.

The discipline of resistance: *Rejoice in the Lord alway: and again I say, Rejoice* (Philippians 4:4). Not the cheap optimism that pretends nothing is wrong, but the rooted joy that knows what God has done, what God is doing, and what God will do — and refuses to be displaced from that knowledge by the political climate of any given week.

## Stronghold II — Dependence as captivity to a parental state

Joan's second item is *dependence on big government*: she frames the captives as needy and scared children, at a loss to function adequately in the adult world.

This is the spirit of *infantile passivity dressed up as compassion*, and it captures human beings by offering them a substitute for the actual work of forming their souls into mature image-bearers of God. The biblical resonance is the murmuring of Israel in the wilderness wishing for the fleshpots of Egypt (Exodus 16:3) — preferring the security of slavery to the responsibility of freedom. Egypt provided. The wilderness required. The captives chose the providing.

The stronghold operates by persuading the human being that *someone else* is responsible for what the human being is responsible for. The someone else can be the state, an ideology, a charismatic leader, a tribal affiliation, a parental figure who refuses to release the adult to adulthood. The mechanism is the same. The captive surrenders the burden of his own moral, economic, intellectual, and spiritual formation to an external agent who agrees to carry it on his behalf, and in exchange surrenders the freedom and dignity that come with bearing the burden himself.

The progressive form of the captivity is the most visible in Joan's framing — the citizen who looks to the state for cradle-to-grave provision, who experiences the suggestion that he might be responsible for himself as cruelty. The conservative form is real but less acknowledged. The conservative captive of this stronghold looks to a charismatic political leader to fix problems the captive should be solving in his own family and community, treats his political tribe as the agent that will deliver the cultural restoration he himself is unwilling to undertake, surrenders his own intellectual responsibility by absorbing his views from his preferred talk-radio host or podcaster rather than thinking them through. The captivity is the same shape. The vehicle is different.

The Democratic Party has, in its policy and rhetorical posture across multiple decades, systematically activated this stronghold among its electoral base. This is empirically observable in the platform documents and the rhetorical strategies; it is not a partisan caricature. *We will take care of you* is a recurring frame in Democratic mobilization, directed precisely at the demographic the stronghold has captured. To the extent this is the party's de facto strategy — and across multiple cycles it has been — the party is functioning as a vehicle for this spirit, organizing the captives, mobilizing them, and rewarding the captivity with policy outcomes that deepen it. This is the institutional accountability the framework requires us to name. The Republican Party has its own institutional patterns that reward the conservative form of the same stronghold — the personality cult of charismatic leaders that requires the follower's continuing surrender of independent judgment, the ideological tribalism that does the moral thinking on behalf of the captive — and these too are named. Both parties have stronghold-aligned mobilization patterns. The framework names both.

The discipline of resistance: the recovery of mature personhood under God. *I am crucified with Christ: nevertheless I live; yet not I, but Christ liveth in me: and the life which I now live in the flesh I live by the faith of the Son of God* (Galatians 2:20). The believer is not infantilized; he has been made *son*, capable of bearing the responsibilities sonship requires. The political agent is not the believer's father. The party is not the believer's family. The state is not the believer's provider. The believer is to grow up into Christ.

## Stronghold III — Infantilization

Joan's third item is *behaving like infants and children*: she points to frequent temper tantrums, public obscenities, and the substitution of theatrical emotion for adult discourse.

This is the same spirit as Stronghold II viewed from a different angle. Where Stronghold II is the structural surrender of adult responsibility, Stronghold III is the *behavioral expression* of the surrender — the public performance of immaturity as a permanent personality state. The two are interlocking; Stronghold II produces Stronghold III, and Stronghold III ratifies Stronghold II.

The biblical witness on what mature adulthood looks like is consistent. *When I was a child, I spake as a child, I understood as a child, I thought as a child: but when I became a man, I put away childish things* (1 Corinthians 13:11). *That we henceforth be no more children, tossed to and fro, and carried about with every wind of doctrine* (Ephesians 4:14). The believer is to grow up. The captivity to perpetual childishness — the inability to disagree without screaming, the inability to lose without melting down, the substitution of emotional intensity for argument, the public performance of feeling as a substitute for the work of thinking — is not a Democratic trait. It is a stronghold operating across the contemporary moment, and it has captured human beings on every political side.

The conservative form deserves direct naming, because Joan's column treats this as something the political opposition does. It is not. The conservative-captive form of Stronghold III includes: the public meltdowns of conservative commentators when their preferred politician is criticized; the inability to engage substantive disagreement without resorting to schoolyard taunts; the elevation of personality-tribal loyalty over policy reasoning; the *I-know-you-are-but-what-am-I* response pattern that has come to characterize substantial portions of conservative social media; the willingness to celebrate any rhetorical cruelty toward perceived enemies as long as it pleases the tribe. These are not progressive failings. They are conservative failings, in mirror image of the progressive failings Joan inventoried, and they are the same stronghold's operations through a different cultural channel.

The institutional vehicles operate on both sides. Progressive activist groups have made tantrum-as-political-tactic a core organizing strategy across multiple decades. Conservative entertainment media has made performative outrage the recurring mode of its product. Both parties activate the stronghold among their captives because the stronghold *works* — emotional intensity, performed grievance, theatrical childishness all generate engagement, donations, electoral mobilization. The institutions reward what the stronghold produces, and so the stronghold deepens its grip. This is what the institutional accountability looks like in practice: the institutions did not invent the stronghold, but they have built financial and organizational structures that reliably activate it, and their activation of it across cycles makes them functional vehicles for it.

The discipline of resistance: *Be not children in understanding: howbeit in malice be ye children, but in understanding be men* (1 Corinthians 14:20). Childlike in trust toward God, in absence of malice toward neighbor, in receptivity to the truth. Adult in capacity to disagree without melting down, to lose without tantrum, to engage difficult questions with sustained patience rather than emotional theater.

## Stronghold IV — Anger as identity

Joan's fourth item is *anger on parade*: she observes that the captives in question seem perpetually angry or aggrieved about something, with the anger never resolving but instead reconstituting itself around new objects as old ones are addressed.

This is the spirit of *perpetual grievance*, and it differs from righteous anger in a specific way. Righteous anger is a response to a specific injustice, oriented toward addressing the specific injustice, and dissolves once the injustice is addressed. Grievance-as-identity is anger that has become the captive's *defining characteristic* — the lens through which every situation is processed, the channel through which every emotion is filtered, the operating frame within which the captive lives. It is not anger about something; it is anger as the *self*. The captive does not have anger; the anger has the captive.

*Be ye angry, and sin not: let not the sun go down upon your wrath* (Ephesians 4:26). Paul allows anger; he limits its duration. The captive of Stronghold IV violates the duration limit by an order of magnitude — the sun has gone down on the same wrath for a decade, and the wrath is no longer wrath about something but the captive's permanent resting state. The scriptural witness on this is consistent. *Cease from anger, and forsake wrath: fret not thyself in any wise to do evil* (Psalm 37:8). *He that is slow to anger is better than the mighty; and he that ruleth his spirit than he that taketh a city* (Proverbs 16:32). *Let all bitterness, and wrath, and anger, and clamour, and evil speaking, be put away from you, with all malice* (Ephesians 4:31). The biblical posture is not pacifist; righteous anger is real and legitimate at the moment. But the captive is not in righteous anger; the captive is in grievance-as-identity, and that is a captivity to be resisted.

This stronghold captures with breathtaking impartiality across the political spectrum. The progressive captive of Stronghold IV processes every news cycle as further evidence of the systemic injustices that justify her permanent rage. The conservative captive of Stronghold IV processes every news cycle as further evidence of the cultural collapse that justifies his permanent rage. The two captives are mirror images. Their rage has the same texture, the same structural logic, the same defining-characteristic role in their personalities. They differ only in their preferred grievance-objects. They are the same stronghold's captures.

The institutional vehicles are by now familiar. Algorithmic social media has been engineered to maximize engagement, and engagement is maximized by activating Stronghold IV in users on every side. Cable news has built business models on the same activation. Political fundraising operations across both parties have discovered that grievance produces donations and have built mailing lists that reliably activate the stronghold to monetize the captive's permanent rage. The captive is, in this institutional ecosystem, the product. His sustained rage is what the institutions monetize. The institutions did not invent his rage, but they have built infrastructure that systematically deepens it, and they reliably reward its expression with the engagement metrics and dollar flows that ratify the captive's identification of himself with the rage. This is institutional accountability for spiritual harm at industrial scale. Both parties' apparatuses participate in it.

The discipline of resistance: rage is allowed at the moment, in response to the specific injustice, oriented toward addressing the specific injustice. Permanent grievance-as-identity is to be put down. The believer's resting state is the rooted peace of Philippians 4:7 — *the peace of God, which passeth all understanding* — not the simmering rage that the algorithms have tried to install as the default mode of his soul.

## Stronghold V — Jealousy

Joan's fifth item names *jealousy* as one of humanity's strongest emotions — second only to fear in her ranking — and locates its operation behind cancel-culture mechanisms and behind the disproportionate hostility toward Israel that some quarters of progressive politics display. The cancel-culture mechanism is, on her reading, envy operationalized at scale.

This is the spirit of *envy*, and the apostolic deposit treats it with unusual seriousness. *For where envying and strife is, there is confusion and every evil work* (James 3:16). *The works of the flesh are manifest, which are these … hatred, variance, emulations, wrath, strife, seditions, heresies, envyings, murders* (Galatians 5:19–21). Envy is named in the same list as murder. The proximity is not rhetorical excess; envy is the operating spirit behind much of what the apostles called the works of the flesh, and the believer who indulges envy has begun the journey toward the rest of the list.

Joan has located one of envy's operations correctly — the cancel-culture mechanism is, on inspection, an envy-driven operation in which the captive cannot tolerate the success or recognition of those whose views or status the captive cannot tolerate, and constructs social mechanisms to destroy what the captive cannot have or be. But Joan has also misidentified envy as a Democratic trait. Envy is one of the most reliably cross-tribal of all the strongholds. The conservative captive of envy who cannot tolerate the cultural status of his progressive neighbors, who imagines that every academic or artistic recognition of a left-leaning figure is a personal affront, who treats the cultural elites' approval of certain views as a wound that must be answered with the destruction of the elites — this is also envy, in mirror form. The two captives recognize each other on social media every day. They are envy's twin captures.

The biblical antidote is *charity envieth not* (1 Corinthians 13:4). The discipline is the cultivation of love that genuinely rejoices in the good of others — including the others whose status, success, recognition, or cultural standing the envious captive's spirit refuses to allow him to celebrate. *Rejoice with them that do rejoice, and weep with them that weep* (Romans 12:15). Both halves are required. The believer who can rejoice with those who rejoice — including those whose rejoicing is over goods the believer would not himself rank as highly — is the believer who has begun to be free of envy.

The Israel question Joan raised deserves a separate note, because she names something genuinely there but in a way the framework asks us to clarify. There is real, sustained, theologically and historically significant hostility toward Israel in some quarters of progressive politics, and the hostility has features that go well beyond ordinary policy disagreement about the Israeli–Palestinian conflict. Some of those features are envy-shaped — the small, thriving, militarily and economically successful nation in a region of failed states draws hostility that policy disagreement alone does not explain. But the hostility also has other ingredients, including absorbed antisemitism with deep historical roots, ideological captivity to anti-colonial frames that are then misapplied to the Jewish state, and a network of disinformation operations that have actively shaped progressive academic and activist discourse on these questions for decades. The framework can name all of this without flattening it into a single emotion. Envy is *part* of what is operating in some quarters of anti-Israel politics. It is not the whole of it. The whole picture is more textured and deserves its own treatment in the political-theology work the framework will undertake in due course.

## Stronghold VI — Victim-identification

Joan's sixth item is *victims R us*: she frames the progressive worldview as one in which the world is divided into helpless victims and cruel victimizers, with conservatives cast in the victimizer role and Democrats positioned as the compassionate saviors who alone can address the injustice.

This is the spirit of *self-identification with victimhood as primary identity*, and it is one of the most spiritually destructive captivities operating in the contemporary moment. The stronghold captures by offering the captive a *narrative role* — the wronged-one, the marginalized-one, the oppressed-one — that becomes the organizing frame of the captive's life. The frame is not negotiable; the captive cannot consider whether he is in fact a victim of what he claims to be a victim of, because the question itself is experienced as further victimization.

The biblical witness on the believer's identity is the opposite. *Behold, what manner of love the Father hath bestowed upon us, that we should be called the sons of God* (1 John 3:1). The believer's primary identity is sonship. Whatever he has suffered — and the believer has suffered, like everyone else — the suffering is not his identity. The suffering is something he has gone through. Sonship is who he is. The believer who has begun to absorb his sonship as identity is no longer captureable by the victim-identification stronghold; he can recognize the genuine wrongs he has experienced without his recognition collapsing into a permanent victim-role.

This stronghold has captured wide swaths of contemporary culture, and Joan's locating it on the progressive side is empirically defensible — the explicit identification of *victim-categories* as the basis for political organization, the scoring of moral and political claims by the victim-status of the claimant, the elevation of *lived experience* of victimhood over considerations of evidence or argument, all of these are observable in contemporary progressive activism in ways they are not symmetrically present in conservative organization. The Democratic Party's electoral coalition has, across multiple decades, been organized substantially around victim-category identification, and the rhetorical mobilization of those categories is a core feature of Democratic political strategy. This institutional pattern is part of what Joan was reaching for, and it deserves the honest naming the framework requires.

But the conservative form of victim-identification is real and increasingly visible. The conservative captive of Stronghold VI sees himself as the victim of cultural elites, the victim of the liberal academy, the victim of mainstream media, the victim of the deep state, the victim of woke corporate America, the victim of an entire social order arrayed against him. The grievances may have substantial truth-content; that is not the question. The question is whether the captive has organized his identity around being the wronged-one — whether his settled posture is the posture of one whose primary self-knowledge is *they are doing things to me*. When that is the operating frame, the conservative is captured by the same stronghold that captures the progressive, in mirror form. The mirror is becoming clearer in conservative discourse year by year.

The discipline of resistance: the recovery of sonship as primary identity. Whatever genuine wrongs the believer has suffered, those wrongs are not his identity. *Wherefore thou art no more a servant, but a son; and if a son, then an heir of God through Christ* (Galatians 4:7). The son is not a victim. The son is loved, equipped, and sent. Wrongs done to the son are addressed, not absorbed.

## Stronghold VII — Conceit

Joan's seventh item is *conceit*: she names the settled assumption among progressives that they are morally and intellectually superior to those they regard as cruel or unenlightened.

Joan has named this stronghold accurately as a real operation, and located one of its visible expressions correctly. The progressive form of conceit — the settled assumption that those who hold progressive cultural positions are *morally and intellectually superior* to those who do not, that disagreement with the progressive consensus on the great moral questions is itself evidence of moral defect — is empirically observable across substantial portions of the contemporary progressive establishment. It produces the credentialed sneer that has become a recognizable feature of progressive cultural production: the assumption that the right to lecture the surrounding country about its moral failings comes by virtue of education, social positioning, and cultural placement, regardless of whether the lecture is grounded in actual wisdom or actual care for the country being lectured.

This is real. Joan saw it.

What Joan did not name — and what the framework requires us to name with equal honesty — is that conservative conceit is the contempt-culture posture diagnosed in [Chapter 5 of the Christos Civitas Code](https://github.com/Renaissance-Ministries/RM/blob/main/CFE_christos_fellowship_essays/book/christos_civitas_code/ch05_component_two_hate_evil_love_captured.md), in mirror form. The conservative captive of Stronghold VII assumes the moral and intellectual superiority of *his* tribe over the cultural opposition, treats every progressive position as evidence of progressive stupidity or malice, dismisses the substantive concerns animating progressive activism as simply *what the dumb people believe*, and elevates his own political tribe to the status of the only people seeing things clearly. The signature is identical to the progressive version. The captives are mirror images of each other, performing the same conceit through different cultural channels.

Joan's column itself is, on this analysis, a worked example. The piece does not engage progressive concerns as serious arguments; it dismisses them as the *toxic ingredients* of a defective tribe whose proper disposition is electoral elimination. This is the conservative form of Stronghold VII operating through Joan's pen. I say this not to attack Joan — Joan is a captured fellow-traveler whose substantive concerns are largely my concerns, and the appropriate posture toward her is the love of the captured that the framework requires. But the captivity is what it is, and naming it is part of the rescue. The same conceit Joan correctly diagnosed in progressives has captured her in mirror form, and the column is the captivity speaking through her.

The biblical witness on conceit is consistent and severe. *Pride goeth before destruction, and an haughty spirit before a fall* (Proverbs 16:18). *Mind not high things, but condescend to men of low estate. Be not wise in your own conceits* (Romans 12:16). *Be of the same mind one toward another. Mind not high things, but condescend to men of low estate. Be not wise in your own conceits* — Paul says it twice in two verses, because the spirit of conceit is so reliably destructive that the warning bears repetition. *God resisteth the proud, but giveth grace unto the humble* (James 4:6). The captive of conceit is, by scriptural testimony, in active resistance from God. This is not a small captivity. This is one of the most spiritually dangerous of all the strongholds, because it positions the captive *above* God's other image-bearers and makes the captive's posture toward them the contempt that God Himself has reserved as His judicial response to the proud.

The discipline of resistance is harder than it looks because the captive does not feel his conceit as conceit; he feels it as *being right*. The diagnostic is in the *posture toward those who disagree*. The believer who is right and humble holds his rightness with grief over those who do not see, with sustained engagement, with willingness to be persuaded where persuasion is warranted, with continuing love for the disagreers. The captive of conceit holds his rightness with contempt for the disagreers, treats their disagreement as evidence of their inferiority, and finds the prospect of their elimination from public discourse rhetorically satisfying. The diagnostic separates the two reliably.

## Stronghold VIII — Intolerance

Joan's eighth item is *intolerance*: she identifies the cancel-culture phenomenon as the operating expression of a settled refusal to host disfavored views in the public square, with the concomitant working to remove their carriers from positions of cultural influence.

This is the operating expression of Stronghold VII. Where conceit assumes superiority, intolerance acts on the assumption by refusing to host the disfavored views in the public square, by working to remove their carriers from positions of cultural influence, and by treating the very expression of the disfavored views as a kind of social violence requiring suppression. The cancel-culture phenomenon Joan names is the most visible institutional expression of this stronghold in contemporary progressive cultural production, and Joan is right to name it.

Joan is also describing, without recognizing she is doing so, the contempt-culture conservative response to the same phenomenon. The conservative captive of Stronghold VIII has, in many cases, built a mirror cancel-culture operation aimed at progressive cultural figures — the public-shaming campaigns directed at progressive academics and journalists, the boycotts and pressure campaigns designed to remove progressive figures from corporate or institutional positions, the celebration of any rhetorical or professional destruction visited on a progressive opponent as a victory for *our side*. The mirror is now well-developed. The conservative who pretends his side does not do this is either uninformed or in active denial.

The biblical command is plain. *Bless them which persecute you: bless, and curse not* (Romans 12:14). *Recompense to no man evil for evil* (Romans 12:17). *Be not overcome of evil, but overcome evil with good* (Romans 12:21). The Christian response to being canceled is not to cancel back; the Christian response to being maligned is to bless. This is not pacifism in the political sense — the believer can resist unjust legal or institutional regimes through every legitimate means, can defend himself in court, can build alternative institutions where the existing institutions have been captured. What he cannot do is *return cancel for cancel*, because that is to be conformed to the very stronghold that has captured his opponent. *Be not conformed to this world: but be ye transformed by the renewing of your mind* (Romans 12:2). The transformation is the believer's calling. Mirroring the captivity is not transformation. It is being conformed to the captivity in mirror image.

The institutional vehicles for Stronghold VIII operate on both sides. Progressive academia and progressive corporate diversity-and-inclusion regimes have built institutional infrastructure that systematically activates and rewards the stronghold's operation in their domain. Conservative activist groups have built parallel infrastructure that does the same in their domain. Both infrastructures are vehicles for the same stronghold. The believer recognizes the vehicles for what they are and refuses to participate in either, even when the targeting of cancellation is being directed at someone the believer would otherwise have reasons to oppose.

The discipline of resistance: *charity suffereth long, and is kind … beareth all things, believeth all things, hopeth all things, endureth all things* (1 Corinthians 13:4–7). The fruit of the Spirit's freedom from this stronghold is the ability to host disagreement in the public square, to engage substantively with views the believer believes are wrong, to allow the wrong views to be aired and answered rather than suppressed, and to extend to the holders of wrong views the dignity of being treated as serious interlocutors rather than as targets for elimination.

## What Joan was reaching for, and what the framework offers her

I have engaged Joan's inventory with the eight strongholds because the inventory she produced, once redirected, is genuinely useful. She catalogued eight characteristic operating modes of demonic captivity in the contemporary moment. Her error was not in the cataloguing; the cataloguing is on its surface accurate. Her error was in the attribution — the assumption that the strongholds are the operating modes of one political tribe, mockable from the secure standpoint of the other tribe whose operating modes are healthy by contrast. They are not. Each of the eight captures human beings on every side of every fault line, and Joan's column is itself a worked example of how Stronghold VII (conceit) and Stronghold VIII (intolerance) operate through conservative cultural production.

The institutional accountability question is real and the framework requires us to name it. The Democratic Party as an organization, across multiple decades and across multiple platform documents and rhetorical strategies, has made systematic appeals to people captured by Strongholds I, II, IV, and VI in particular, and the party's electoral coalition is mobilized substantially through the activation of those captivities. This is empirically observable; it is not a partisan caricature. The party functions, on this analysis, as an institutional vehicle for those particular spirits whether or not any individual member of the party intends this. This is the institutional accountability the framework names.

The Republican Party as an organization, across the same decades, has made systematic appeals to people captured by Strongholds VII and VIII in particular — the contempt-culture posture and the mirror-cancellation infrastructure — and increasingly by Strongholds I and IV in their conservative forms. This is also empirically observable. The party functions, on this analysis, as an institutional vehicle for *its* set of strongholds whether or not any individual member intends this. The framework names this with equal honesty.

The framework does not therefore conclude that *both parties are equally bad and the believer should despair of politics*. That is not what it concludes. What it concludes is that the believer's political engagement — voting, organizing, advocacy, civic participation — should be undertaken with eyes open to which strongholds each party is currently activating, with willingness to support the party whose substantive policy commitments are closer to the apostolic deposit on the issues where the deposit speaks clearly, and with refusal to be assimilated into either party's stronghold-activation strategy. The believer is in the country, voting in the country's elections, advocating for policies that conform to the apostolic deposit's witness — but he is not *of* either party, in the spiritual sense, because both parties are functioning, in different ways, as vehicles for spirits that he is committed to resisting.

To Joan, if she ever sees this: I am with you on most of what your column was reaching for. The patterns you observed in contemporary Democratic Party rhetoric and policy are real. The substantive concerns about the genocidal-violence question regarding Hamas, about the historical record of the Democratic Party on civil rights, about the dependency structures created by certain progressive policy designs, about the cultural revolution operating through institutional channels — these are concerns I share. What I am asking the framework to do, with your inventory, is *relocate the diagnosis* from the level of *Democrats* to the level of *strongholds that capture human beings*. The patterns you saw are real; they are not Democratic; they are demonic. Some of them have made the Democratic Party an institutional vehicle for them. Some of them have made the Republican Party an institutional vehicle for different ones. The believer is bound to neither vehicle. The believer is bound to Christ, to the apostolic deposit, to the love of the captured on every side, and to the resistance of the spirits regardless of which institutional vehicles are currently carrying them.

To the fellowship: this is what the framework looks like applied to a piece of polemical conservative cultural commentary. The substantive concerns that animated the column are not being abandoned. They are being relocated, named more precisely, and addressed in a way that does not require us to mirror the captivity-postures of the people whose captivities Joan was attempting to name. We hate the spirits. We love the captured on every side. We listen carefully and respond to what is actually there. We keep the certainty/strong-inference/prudential taxonomy straight. We refuse to be assimilated into the stronghold-activation strategies of either political party. We hold our convictions firmly and our relationships open. *One heart to make Christ King.*
