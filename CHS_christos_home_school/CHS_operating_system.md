## 1. Purpose

### 1.1 What This Template Does

This template is the operational core of the Christos Home School (CHS) module. It takes any lecture, textbook chapter, article, or educational material and processes it through the Christos framework to produce a structured study guide.

The study guide equips the student to:
- Understand the source material on its own terms.
- Identify the embedded assumptions and frameworks operating in the source.
- Evaluate the source's claims against Kingdom principles.
- Retain what is true and useful while recognizing what is false, incomplete, or misleading.
- Articulate a Kingdom-grounded perspective on the same topic.

### 1.2 What This Template Is NOT

This template does not:
- Replace the source material. The student should engage the original lecture or text first.
- Provide a "correct answer" to memorize. It provides a framework for thinking.
- Dismiss all secular education as worthless. Most sources contain genuine knowledge worth learning.
- Turn every lecture into a theological debate. Some material is primarily technical and needs only minor Kingdom commentary.

### 1.3 Design Principles

**Proportional Response:** The depth of Kingdom analysis should match the degree of worldview content in the source. A lecture on supply and demand mechanics needs light commentary. A lecture arguing that capitalism is inherently exploitative needs thorough engagement.

**Intellectual Honesty:** The CHS student should be able to represent the source's arguments accurately before critiquing them. Straw men are forbidden. The goal is to be a better thinker than the source, not a louder one.

**Actionable Output:** Every study guide should leave the student with something concrete — knowledge retained, assumptions identified, questions to pursue, a Kingdom perspective articulated.

## 5. Phase 2: Claim Extraction

### 5.1 Claim Types

Read or listen to the source material carefully and extract claims into four categories:

**Factual Claims:** Assertions about what happened, what exists, or what is the case. These are verifiable in principle.
- Example: "The Federal Reserve was established in 1913."
- Example: "Unemployment reached 25% during the Great Depression."

**Causal Claims:** Assertions about why something happened — what caused what. These involve interpretation.
- Example: "The credit expansion of the 1920s caused the Great Depression."
- Example: "The New Deal ended the Depression."
- Example: "Free trade caused the decline of American manufacturing."

**Normative Claims:** Assertions about what should be, what is good or bad, what is just or unjust. These involve values.
- Example: "The gold standard was a barbarous relic."
- Example: "Free markets produce the most prosperity."
- Example: "Inequality is the central economic problem."

**Methodological Claims:** Assertions about how we should study or think about the topic. These are about epistemology and method.
- Example: "Economic laws can be deduced from the axiom of human action." (Austrian)
- Example: "Only testable, empirical hypotheses constitute genuine knowledge." (Positivist)
- Example: "History has no laws; each episode is contingent." (Historicist)

### 5.2 Omission Identification

Equally important to what the source says is what it does not say. After extracting explicit claims, ask:

- **What facts are omitted?** What relevant historical events, data, or context does the source leave out?
- **What perspectives are omitted?** Whose voice is absent? What alternative interpretations are not mentioned?
- **What moral dimensions are omitted?** Does the source treat a moral question as merely technical, or ignore the moral dimension entirely?
- **What alternatives are omitted?** Does the source discuss what was happening elsewhere or what replaced the arrangement it describes?

## 7. Phase 4: Kingdom Analysis

### 7.1 CHR Five-Part Framework Application

Apply the CHR's Five-Part Framework to the topic covered by the source:

**The Standard Established:** What does Kingdom culture require in this domain? (Cite specific CEA Kingdom Economic Standard principles if economic.)

**The Aspiration Acknowledged:** What were the historical actors trying to accomplish? (Give them fair hearing before evaluating.)

**The Execution Evaluated:** Where did they succeed or fail against the Kingdom standard? (Honest assessment — acknowledge both.)

**The Alternatives Assessed:** What was happening elsewhere? What were the realistic alternatives? What replaced this arrangement?

**The Lessons Extracted:** What should the student carry forward? What principles should guide Kingdom culture in this domain?

### 7.2 CEA-Specific Analysis (if applicable)

**Cui Bono:** For each institution or policy the source describes, complete a Cui Bono analysis (CEA Section 5).

**Flourishing Assessment:** For any claim of "progress" or "improvement," complete the seven-dimension Flourishing Assessment (CEA Section 6).

**Honest Weights Check:** Does the monetary or financial arrangement described involve manipulation of the medium of exchange? If so, identify the mechanism and the beneficiaries.

**School Alignment/Divergence:** Using the CEA Economic Schools Reference Grid, identify where the source's preferred school aligns with and diverges from Kingdom principles.

### 7.3 The Two-Column Evaluation

For each major claim or argument in the source, produce a two-column evaluation:

| Source's Argument | Kingdom Evaluation |
|------------------|--------------------|
| [State the argument fairly] | [Evaluate against Kingdom standard: agree, disagree, agree-but-extend, agree-on-facts-but-reject-framework, etc.] |

### 7.4 Proportional Response Check

Before proceeding to the study guide, verify:

- Is the depth of analysis proportional to the worldview content of the source?
- Has the source been represented fairly before being evaluated?
- Have valid insights been acknowledged, even in contested sources?

### Section A: Summary

This introductory lecture establishes the scope and method of the course. Woods defines economic history as regular history with emphasis on production, exchange, and consumption. He distinguishes two genres: microeconomic history (entrepreneurs, industries, technologies) and macroeconomic history (institutions, economic progress, crises).

The bulk of the lecture surveys competing methods of doing economic history. Woods rejects three approaches: determinism (Marx's teleological materialism, Marshall's Victorian progressivism, Keynes' state-accelerated utopia), historicism (Schmoller's denial of universal economic laws), and scientism/positivism (Friedman's model-building, North and Fogel's cliometrics). He adopts Ludwig von Mises' "thymology" — the blending of universal economic laws (discovered by deduction) with contingent historical specifics. He illustrates thymology's three problems using competing explanations of the Great Depression (Friedman's monetary contraction vs. Rothbard's prior credit expansion).

The lecture concludes by outlining the three macro pillars the course will trace: institutions of the market economy (property, contracts, enterprise, monetary system), the theory of economic progress (resources, technology, entrepreneurship, division of labor, capital accumulation, time preference), and the Austrian theory of the business cycle (boom → crisis → bust → recovery).

---

### Section B: Key Claims

**Factual Claims:**
1. Economic history addresses production, exchange, and consumption within the broader study of human action. — *Verified.*
2. Menger and Schmoller debated in the Methodenstreit over whether universal economic laws exist. — *Verified.*
3. Milton Friedman emphasizes monetary deflation as the key cause of the Great Depression; Murray Rothbard emphasizes prior credit expansion. — *Verified.*
4. North and Fogel called their positivist approach "cliometrics." — *Verified.*

**Causal Claims:**
1. Credit expansion lowers interest rates, causes malinvestment, and produces the boom-bust cycle. — *Consistent with Austrian theory; Kingdom analysis extends: this is also a "divers weights" violation.*
2. Economic progress is caused by resource improvement, technology, entrepreneurship, institutional quality, division of labor, and capital accumulation. — *Broadly valid; Kingdom analysis extends: genuine flourishing requires more than material progress.*
3. Market economies function when property, contracts, enterprise, and sound money are legally upheld. — *Consistent with Kingdom stewardship principles.*

**Normative Claims:**
1. Thymology is the correct method for doing economic history. — *Strongest of the options presented; Kingdom analysis: compatible with biblical epistemology because it affirms both universal principles and human agency, but needs moral supplementation.*
2. Determinism, historicism, and positivism are inadequate methods. — *Largely agreed; Kingdom analysis notes that determinism's denial of human agency also denies moral accountability.*

**Methodological Claims:**
1. Universal economic laws exist and can be deduced from the axiom of human action. — *Partially aligned with Kingdom view: natural law reasoning affirms discoverable order in creation. But praxeology's value-neutrality is a limitation.*
2. History is made by purposive individuals, not impersonal forces. — *Strongly aligned with Kingdom anthropology: humans are moral agents, not cogs in material-historical processes.*
3. Concrete historical explanation requires blending theory with contingent elements. — *Sound methodology; applicable beyond economics to all CHR analysis.*

---

### Section C: Framework Analysis

**Economic School:** Austrian (Mises-Rothbard tradition)  
**Historical Method:** Thymological  
**Anthropological Assumptions:** Human beings are purposive actors employing means to achieve subjectively valued ends. Choices cannot be fully reduced to material cause and effect.  
**Moral Framework:** Methodologically value-neutral. Woods does not evaluate whether the ends people pursue are good or bad — only how they act to pursue them.

**Kingdom Assessment:** Woods' Austrian-thymological framework is the most hospitable secular methodology for Kingdom analysis. Its affirmation of human agency, universal economic laws, individual purposiveness, and the reality of unintended consequences all resonate with biblical anthropology and natural law reasoning. Its main limitation is precisely what makes it "secular" — it deliberately excludes moral evaluation of the ends people pursue and the justice of the institutional arrangements they create. This is where the CEA supplies what Austrian economics, by methodological choice, omits.

---

### Section D: Kingdom Perspective

Woods' lecture is a masterclass in methodological clearing of the ground. Before telling any history, he equips the student with the tools to think about history correctly. From a Kingdom perspective, this is exactly right — method precedes content, just as theology precedes ethics.

The four methods Woods surveys map onto a deeper theological question: What kind of world do we live in, and what kind of beings are we?

Determinism (Marx, Hegel) says we live in a world driven by impersonal forces, and we are instruments of those forces. This is incompatible with the biblical picture at every level. God is personal, not an impersonal dialectic. Human beings are moral agents, not class-determined automata. History has a purpose, but that purpose is God's, worked out through — not despite — human freedom and accountability.

Historicism (Schmoller) says there are no universal laws, only contingent circumstances. This is a denial of natural law — the very order in creation that Scripture affirms. Economic laws like supply and demand are not human inventions; they are regularities in created human nature that God has embedded in the way the world works. Denying them is like denying gravity — you don't escape the law, you merely fail to understand why you hit the ground.

Positivism (Friedman, North, Fogel) says only empirically testable hypotheses count as knowledge. This is a philosophical claim that refutes itself — it is not itself empirically testable. It also excludes the entire domain of moral knowledge, which is precisely the domain Kingdom analysis requires. You cannot evaluate whether an economic arrangement is just if your epistemology only admits empirical hypotheses.

Thymology (Mises, Rothbard) affirms universal laws, human agency, purposive action, and the blending of theory with concrete specifics. This is the closest secular method to how a Kingdom historian should work — because it mirrors the structure of biblical wisdom: eternal principles (God's revealed law) applied to concrete circumstances (the prudential judgments of life).

Where Woods' framework stops short — and where the CHS student must go further — is in the content of the universal principles. For Mises, the "axiom of human action" is value-neutral: humans act purposively, and that's all we can say a priori. For the Kingdom student, we can say much more: humans are image-bearers who are called to stewardship, whose actions are morally accountable, who live under a revealed standard of justice, and whose economic arrangements will be evaluated not only by whether they "work" in producing material output but by whether they honor God, serve the vulnerable, maintain honest measures, and enable genuine human flourishing.

This is not a criticism of Woods — he is doing economics, not theology, and he does it well. It is a recognition that economics without theology is like anatomy without physiology: you can describe the parts accurately but you cannot say what the body is *for*.

The three macro pillars Woods identifies — institutions, progress, and crises — are exactly the right categories. The CEA extends each one: institutions are evaluated by the Kingdom Economic Standard (CEA Section 3), progress is evaluated by the Flourishing Assessment (CEA Section 6), and crises are understood not only as economic dislocations but as the consequences of violating the "honest weights" principle through monetary manipulation.

---

### Section E: What to Retain / What to Flag

**Retain:**
- The thymological method — blending universal laws with contingent specifics. This is the right way to do history and should be adopted as the CHS analytical default.
- The micro/macro distinction in economic history. Useful organizational framework.
- The three macro pillars: institutions, progress, crises. These structure the entire course and map well onto CEA analysis.
- The Austrian theory of the business cycle as the leading explanation of economic crises. The CEA extends this with the "honest weights" analysis.
- The critique of determinism, historicism, and positivism. All three are inadequate from both economic and theological perspectives.
- Thymology's three problems (selection of relevant elements, weighting, and historical concept formation) — directly applicable to CHR analysis.

**Flag:**
- Value-neutrality. Woods (following Mises) treats all human purposes as analytically equivalent. Kingdom analysis does not — some purposes are righteous, some are wicked, and this matters for evaluating historical outcomes.
- No moral evaluation of institutions. Woods will describe whether property was privately held or not, whether money was sound or debased — but he will not say "this was unjust." The CEA must supply that evaluation.
- Progress defined materially. "Rising standards of living" means more and better consumer goods per capita. The Flourishing Assessment adds the other six dimensions.
- Absence of provision-for-the-vulnerable analysis. Austrian economics has no internal mechanism for evaluating whether an economic arrangement serves or fails the widow, orphan, and stranger.

**Extend:**
- The thymological method should be extended to include moral law alongside economic law in the set of "universal principles" that are blended with contingent specifics.
- Each institution Woods describes should be evaluated not only for economic function but for Kingdom justice (Cui Bono analysis).
- The business cycle analysis should be extended with the "honest weights" frame — credit expansion is not merely an economic error but a moral one.

### Section F: Discussion Questions

1. **Comprehension:** Woods describes four methods of doing economic history. Can you summarize each one and explain why he rejects three of them?

2. **Analysis:** Woods follows Mises in claiming that there are universal economic laws discoverable by deduction. How does this compare to the biblical concept of natural law — the idea that God has embedded discoverable order in creation?

3. **Evaluation:** Mises' thymology is deliberately value-neutral — it describes how people act but does not evaluate whether their purposes are good or bad. What are the strengths of this approach? What are its limitations from a Kingdom perspective?

4. **Application:** Woods says that one of thymology's key challenges is selecting the "relevant contingent elements" for explaining a historical event. How does this apply to understanding current events? Pick a current economic issue and identify which "contingent elements" different people would select as most important, and why.

5. **Extension:** Woods describes "economic progress" as rising material standards of living. Using the CEA Flourishing Assessment (Section 6), what other dimensions of flourishing should we consider when evaluating whether a society is truly progressing?

6. **Scripture Connection:** Read Deuteronomy 25:13-15 (honest weights and measures). How does this principle apply to the monetary system institutions that Woods will be examining throughout the course?

7. **Kingdom Synthesis:** If you were designing an economic history course from a Kingdom perspective, what would you add to Woods' three macro pillars (institutions, progress, crises) that his Austrian framework cannot provide?

## 11. AI Operating Instructions

### 11.1 Processing a New Lecture

When a student provides a transcript and requests processing, the AI should:

1. **Confirm the source.** Identify the course, lecture number, and author.
2. **Classify the source.** Allied, contested, mixed, or neutral-technical.
3. **Determine CEA applicability.** Does the source make economic claims?
4. **Execute Phase 1 through Phase 5** in sequence.
5. **Produce the study guide** in the format specified in Sections 8 and 9.
6. **Apply proportional response.** Don't over-analyze neutral-technical content; don't under-analyze contested worldview content.

### 11.2 Quality Standards

Every AI-produced study guide must:

- Represent the source's arguments accurately and fairly before evaluating them.
- Identify the embedded framework explicitly, with evidence from the text.
- Cite specific CEA Kingdom Economic Standard principles by number (3.1 through 3.7) where applicable.
- Include at least one Cui Bono analysis if the source describes an institution or policy.
- Include a Flourishing Assessment if the source claims "progress."
- Provide discussion questions at multiple cognitive levels (comprehension through synthesis).
- Maintain a tone that is confident, honest, and respectful — not dismissive, not defensive, not propagandistic.

### 11.3 Consistency Across Study Guides

The AI should maintain consistency in:
- File naming conventions (Section 9.1)
- Metadata format (Section 9.4)
- Study guide section structure (Section 8.1)
- Terminology (use CEA and CHR terms consistently)
- Classification criteria (don't reclassify the same source differently across lectures unless the content warrants it)

### 11.4 Handling Edge Cases

**Source contains factual errors:** Note them in Section B with correction and citation.

**Source is excellent and needs minimal Kingdom commentary:** Say so. A short study guide that says "this is solid; here's the one extension" is better than a bloated one that manufactures critique.

**Source is deeply problematic:** Don't spend the entire study guide in attack mode. State the problems clearly, extract whatever valid content exists, and provide the Kingdom alternative. The student should finish the guide knowing more, not just feeling more opposed.

**Student asks for the study guide to be shorter or longer:** Adjust proportionally while maintaining all required sections. Sections can be condensed but not eliminated.

**Multiple sources on the same topic:** Cross-reference. Note where sources agree, where they diverge, and where the Kingdom perspective differs from both.

### Appendix B: Common Framework Indicators

**Indicators of Keynesian Framework:**
- Language about "aggregate demand," "stimulus," "multiplier effect"
- Assumption that government spending drives recovery
- Treatment of saving as potentially harmful ("paradox of thrift")
- Emphasis on unemployment as the primary economic problem
- Approval of deficit spending and monetary expansion

**Indicators of Marxist/Progressive Framework:**
- Language about "exploitation," "surplus value," "class conflict"
- Assumption that capitalism is inherently unjust
- Treatment of inequality as the primary economic problem
- Emphasis on structural/systemic explanations over individual agency
- Approval of state ownership or heavy regulation

**Indicators of Austrian Framework:**
- Language about "human action," "subjective value," "malinvestment"
- Emphasis on individual choice and entrepreneurship
- Treatment of government intervention as the primary cause of economic problems
- Skepticism of central banking and fiat money
- Emphasis on the business cycle as a monetary phenomenon

**Indicators of Positivist/Mainstream Framework:**
- Heavy reliance on statistical models and econometric analysis
- Treatment of economics as a predictive science analogous to physics
- Emphasis on "data-driven" conclusions
- Tendency to treat policy as a technical optimization problem
- Avoidance of normative claims (while implicitly embedding them)

### Appendix C: Cognitive Level Guide for Discussion Questions

| Level | Verb | Purpose | Example |
|-------|------|---------|---------|
| Comprehension | Summarize, describe, explain | Can the student restate the source's argument? | "What are the four methods of economic history Woods discusses?" |
| Analysis | Compare, identify, distinguish | Can the student dissect the source's framework? | "What assumptions distinguish thymology from positivism?" |
| Evaluation | Assess, evaluate, judge | Can the student apply Kingdom standards? | "Does Mises' value-neutrality serve or hinder Kingdom analysis?" |
| Application | Apply, demonstrate, use | Can the student use the frameworks in new contexts? | "Apply the Cui Bono framework to a current policy debate." |
| Synthesis | Integrate, design, propose | Can the student create Kingdom analysis independently? | "How would you structure a Kingdom-grounded economic history course?" |
| Scripture Connection | Connect, relate, ground | Can the student root the analysis in Scripture? | "How does Deuteronomy 25 apply to modern monetary policy?" |
