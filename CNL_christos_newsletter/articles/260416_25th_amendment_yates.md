# The 25th Amendment: Can a President Be Removed for Bad Judgment?

## Featured Article

**"Invoking the 25th?"** by Steven Yates, PhD
*Navigating the New Normal* (Substack)
Published April 10, 2026

**Read the full article:** [https://stevenyates.substack.com](https://stevenyates.substack.com)

---

## Summary

Steven Yates, a philosopher and political commentator based in Chile, examines the growing calls — from both former Trump Republicans and Democrats — to invoke the 25th Amendment to remove President Trump from office. The calls intensified after Trump's Easter Sunday social media posts threatening to destroy Iran's infrastructure and "send them back to the stone ages," followed by a rapid reversal toward negotiation and deal-making over the Strait of Hormuz.

Yates walks through the mechanics of the 25th Amendment, Section 4, and concludes that removal is practically impossible in the current political environment. His key points:

**The process is designed to be difficult.** The Vice President must initiate it. A majority of the Cabinet must agree. The President can immediately contest it. Congress then has 21 days to decide by a two-thirds vote in both houses. None of these steps is likely to succeed given current political dynamics.

**Vance would be unlikely to initiate.** His political career would effectively end. He would be seen as a betrayer by Trump's base. And even if he did, controlling the executive branch during the resulting power struggle would be enormously difficult.

**Congress cannot act quickly enough.** Yates notes that Congress couldn't reopen the government during a shutdown of similar length and has done nothing substantive to check the President's military actions. A 21-day constitutional crisis would be chaotic.

**Yates's conclusion:** "We're stuck with Trump for the next almost-three years." He goes further, suggesting (via a friend's theory) that Trump may be a "globalist asset" whose real mission was to contain the dissident right and set the global economy on a course toward severe disruption.

## Analytical Commentary

### What Yates Gets Right

His constitutional analysis is accurate. The 25th Amendment Section 4 was designed for cases of clear physical incapacity — a president in a coma, under anesthesia, suffering a stroke. It was not designed to adjudicate disputes about whether a president's judgment, temperament, or policy decisions are sound. Attempting to use it for that purpose would create a constitutional crisis with no clear resolution mechanism.

His practical political analysis is also sound. In the current Congress, with the current party dynamics, and with Trump's demonstrated willingness to fight any challenge with maximum aggression, the 25th Amendment path is a dead end. This is the consensus view of constitutional scholars across the political spectrum, not a partisan observation.

### What Yates Gets Debatable

Yates presents Trump's social media posts as evidence of emotional instability. This is one interpretation. Another — held by Trump's supporters and by some foreign policy analysts — is that the threat-then-deal pattern is a deliberate negotiating strategy: establish a maximalist position, demonstrate willingness to use extreme force, then offer a deal that looks reasonable by comparison. Whether this constitutes "instability" or "strategy" depends heavily on one's prior assessment of Trump's character and competence.

The claim that Trump is a "globalist asset" undertaking covert missions to destroy the economy and contain the dissident right is a conspiratorial interpretation that Yates attributes to "a friend" rather than defending directly. This framing should be recognized for what it is: speculation presented without evidence, offered as one possible explanation among others. It may or may not be true, but it is not established by the evidence Yates presents.

Yates's characterization of Israel as potentially "the most violent nation on the planet" is a moral judgment that many would contest. Israel operates in a security environment that includes neighbors and non-state actors committed to its destruction. Whether its military actions constitute proportionate self-defense or disproportionate aggression is one of the most contested questions in international affairs. Yates presents only one side.

### What Yates Doesn't Address

The article does not consider whether the constitutional system is working as designed — that is, whether the difficulty of removing a president is a feature, not a bug. The founders made removal deliberately hard because they feared that easy removal would destabilize the republic and make the presidency subject to congressional whims. The very frustration Yates expresses may be evidence that the system is functioning as intended: protecting the office from removal based on policy disagreements, even severe ones.

The article also does not address the role of elections as the constitutional remedy for presidential behavior that the people find unacceptable. The founders' primary answer to "What do we do about a bad president?" was: vote him out. Trump cannot run again in 2028 (he is term-limited). The constitutional system anticipates that the people will endure leaders they disagree with for defined periods, and that this endurance is preferable to the instability of easy removal.

## The Christos Analysis

### What Does Scripture Say About Rulers We Disagree With?

The Bible presents a complex and sometimes paradoxical picture of the citizen's relationship to governing authority:

**Romans 13:1-2** — *"Let every soul be subject unto the higher powers. For there is no power but of God: the powers that be are ordained of God. Whosoever therefore resisteth the power, resisteth the ordinance of God."*

This passage is often cited as requiring obedience to all governing authority. But it must be read in context. Paul wrote this under Nero — one of the most brutal rulers in Roman history. Paul was not endorsing Nero's character or policies. He was articulating a principle of social order: that government as an institution is ordained by God, even when specific governments fall far short of godly standards.

**Acts 5:29** — *"We ought to obey God rather than men."*

Peter and the apostles said this when ordered by the governing authority (the Sanhedrin) to stop preaching. They disobeyed — and accepted the consequences. This establishes the principle that obedience to human authority has limits: when human law directly contradicts God's commands, the Christian must obey God and bear the cost.

**Proverbs 29:2** — *"When the righteous are in authority, the people rejoice: but when the wicked beareth rule, the people mourn."*

This is descriptive, not prescriptive. It tells us what happens, not what to do about it. But it implies that the character of rulers matters — that righteous governance produces flourishing and wicked governance produces suffering. The citizen's responsibility is to work toward righteous governance through legitimate means.

**Jeremiah 29:7** — *"Seek the peace of the city whither I have caused you to be carried away captives, and pray unto the LORD for it: for in the peace thereof shall ye have peace."*

God told the Jewish exiles in Babylon — living under a pagan king who had destroyed their temple — to seek the welfare of the city and pray for it. Even under the worst governance imaginable, the people of God are called to be constructive, not destructive.

### How Does This Apply to the Current Situation?

The Christos framework, as developed in the Kingdom Citizen essay, establishes five duties for the citizen:

1. **Know the law** — Understand both the Constitution and God's moral law. Yates's article is valuable here: it educates citizens about how the 25th Amendment actually works, dispelling both false hopes and false fears.

2. **Judge the law** — Assess whether current governance conforms to constitutional and moral standards. Citizens should form their own judgments about whether Trump's actions are within acceptable bounds — not outsource that judgment to commentators on either side.

3. **Obey or disobey** — Comply with righteous governance; resist unrighteous governance through legitimate means. The 25th Amendment is a legitimate constitutional mechanism, but Yates is correct that it is not practically available in this situation. Other legitimate means remain: congressional oversight, judicial review, public advocacy, and elections.

4. **Bear the cost** — Accept the consequences of principled positions. Those who publicly criticize the President will face social and possibly professional consequences. Those who publicly support him will face different consequences. Both must be willing to stand and bear the cost.

5. **Mobilize action** — Work collectively for reform. This is where the Voting Network concept becomes relevant: rather than simply being frustrated by events, citizens can register their positions, contribute their reasoning, and build a collective body of informed opinion that carries weight with legislators.

### The Deeper Question

Yates asks whether Trump should be removed. The Christos framework asks a different question: **What kind of governance does God require, and what is the citizen's role in pursuing it?**

The answer is not reducible to "support Trump" or "oppose Trump." It is: understand the issues, apply biblical and constitutional principles, form your own judgment, register that judgment publicly with your name attached, and work through legitimate channels to move governance toward righteousness.

The 25th Amendment may not be the right tool. But the citizen's duty to know, to judge, to speak, and to act does not depend on having the right tool. It depends on having the right heart.

## Discussion Questions for Your Fellowship Group

1. **Romans 13 vs. Acts 5:29** — How do you reconcile the command to submit to governing authorities with the command to obey God rather than men? Where is the line? Is it possible to submit to a government you believe is acting wrongly?

2. **The 25th Amendment** — Before reading this article, did you understand how the 25th Amendment actually works? Has your view of its applicability changed after reading Yates's analysis?

3. **Trump's negotiating style** — Yates interprets Trump's threat-then-deal pattern as instability. Others interpret it as strategy. How do you read it? Does the Bible offer guidance on whether threatening destruction to achieve a deal is morally acceptable, even if it works?

4. **Israel and proportionality** — Yates characterizes Israel's military actions as disproportionate. Others defend them as necessary self-defense. What biblical principles apply to the use of military force? Is there a Christian position on proportionality in warfare?

5. **"We're stuck"** — Yates concludes that Americans are stuck with Trump for three more years. Is this resignation appropriate? What does the Christos framework suggest citizens should do when they cannot change their leadership but disagree with its direction?

6. **Conspiatorial thinking** — Yates presents the theory that Trump is a "globalist asset." How should Christians evaluate conspiracy theories? What is the difference between healthy skepticism of official narratives and unhealthy conspiratorial thinking? What does "test all things, hold fast what is good" (1 Thessalonians 5:21) look like in practice?

7. **Your position** — Having read the article, the commentary, and the Christos analysis, where do you stand on the question of presidential accountability? Register your position — not as "pro-Trump" or "anti-Trump," but as a specific, reasoned, biblically-grounded judgment on the specific issues raised.
